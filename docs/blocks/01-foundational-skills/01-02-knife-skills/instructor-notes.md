# 🎓 Instructor Notes — Block 1–2: Knife Skills & Kitchen Setup

> **What this is:** Session-by-session teaching notes — the stuff a chef instructor would say while walking the line. Success criteria, common mistakes, drills, and the insights that build intuition. Use alongside the [Full Guide](index.md).

---

## Block 1 — Grip & Motion

---

### Session 1 — The Pinch Grip

**What Success Looks Like:**

- Your thumb and forefinger are pinching the blade itself — not the bolster, not the handle. If someone pulled the knife, those two fingers would be the last to let go.
- The knife feels top-heavy and awkward. That's correct. If it feels natural on night one, you're probably holding it wrong.
- Your wrist is doing most of the steering, not your shoulder or elbow. Small movements, close to the board.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Hand migrates back to the handle after 2–3 cuts | Muscle memory from years of handle grip | Set a timer for 5 minutes. Every time it goes off, check your grip. You'll catch yourself. |
| Death grip — white knuckles on the handle | Anxiety about control | Loosen your back three fingers. They're just resting on the handle, not gripping it. The pinch does the work. |
| Chopping with the wrist locked, arm doing all the work | Treating the knife like a hammer | Rock the blade — tip stays near the board, heel lifts and drops. Your wrist is the hinge. |

**Drill (2 minutes, before you start cooking):**

Take one clove of garlic and a few sprigs of parsley. Pinch-grip the knife and mince both — slowly, deliberately. Focus only on keeping your thumb and forefinger on the blade the entire time. If your hand drifts back to the handle, stop and reset. This is the only thing you're practicing tonight. The minced garlic goes into your pasta. Nothing wasted.

**Instructor's Insight:**

> The pinch grip doesn't feel better at first — it feels worse. That's the point. The handle grip is comfortable because it keeps your hand far from the blade. The pinch grip puts your hand closer to the cutting edge, which is exactly why it gives you more control. You steer a car from the steering wheel, not from the back seat. Same principle. Give it three sessions before you judge it.

### Session 2 — The Claw

**What Success Looks Like:**

- Your fingertips are curled under, never extended. The flat of your knuckles — not your fingertips — is what the blade slides against.
- The knife moves at the pace your guide hand sets. If you speed up the knife without speeding up the feed, you'll start cutting air or cutting crooked.
- Your carrot and celery pieces are within ½" of each other. Not identical — just not wildly different. No piece should be twice the size of another.
- You keep the tip of the blade on or near the board during the cut, using it as a pivot.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Fingertips poking out past the knuckles | You're watching the knife, not your guide hand | Tuck harder than feels natural. The knuckles are the bumper rail — the blade can't reach your fingers if they're behind that line. |
| Lifting the entire blade off the board between cuts | Treating each cut as a separate chop | Keep the tip down. The blade rocks — tip stays, heel lifts and drops. This gives you a rhythm. |
| Pieces come out wildly different sizes | Moving the guide hand inconsistently | Slow down. Match every cut to the same feed distance: slide the guide hand the same amount between each stroke. Speed comes later; consistency comes first. |

**Instructor's Insight:**

> Your guide hand does more work than your knife hand. The knife follows the knuckles — if the knuckles move at a consistent pace, the cuts come out even. Stop watching the blade. Start paying attention to the hand that's feeding the food. That one adjustment will improve your cuts more than anything else in this block.

### Session 3 — Rock Chop vs. Push Cut

**What Success Looks Like:**

- You can feel the difference between the two strokes. The rock chop has a rhythm — tip anchored, heel rocks. The push cut lifts and presses clean through. They produce different textures in the mince.
- Your garlic is actually minced — a fine, wet paste, not rough chunks. If you can see distinct pieces larger than a grain of rice, keep going.
- Your parsley is fine and uniform, not bruised into a wet green smear. A sharp knife cuts clean; a dull one crushes.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| The garlic scatters across the board | Clove wasn't crushed first, or board is too small | Crush the clove with the flat of the blade first to break the skin and flatten it slightly. Then mince. Scrape the pile back to center every 10–15 strokes. |
| Parsley turns brown/black at the edges after 10 minutes | Knife is dull — it's tearing, not cutting | Hone the knife. If honing doesn't help, it needs sharpening. Bruised herbs oxidize fast. Clean cuts stay green. |
| Using only one stroke for everything | Both strokes feel the same when you're new | Slow down and exaggerate the difference. Rock chop: tip glued to the board, heel pivots. Push cut: entire blade lifts, pushes straight down and forward. Hold each position for a beat before cutting so you can feel the distinction. |

**Instructor's Insight:**

> Here's a test for your mince: take a pinch of your minced garlic and rub it between your thumb and forefinger. If you feel distinct chunks, it's a rough chop, not a mince. A real mince feels almost like a paste. That texture difference changes how garlic behaves in a hot pan — fine mince melts into a sauce in 30 seconds. Chunks burn on the outside while staying raw inside.

### Session 4 — Mise en Place

**What Success Looks Like:**

- Every single ingredient is prepped, measured, and visible before you turn on the burner. Protein sliced and marinated. Sauce mixed. Aromatics chopped. Rice already cooking.
- When you start the stir-fry, your only job is to cook. You're not reaching for the cornstarch, not scrambling to mince ginger, not opening bottles. Everything is already there.
- The actual stir-fry takes 4–6 minutes. If it takes longer, your mise wasn't complete.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Starting to cook before everything is prepped | Impatience, or underestimating prep time | Physically turn your stove off until mise is complete. Make it a rule for this session: burner stays cold until the last ingredient is prepped. |
| Beef comes out gray and steamed instead of seared | Pan wasn't hot enough, or too much beef at once | Read the "No wok?" note in the full guide. Cook in 2–3 batches, 4–5 pieces per batch. The pan must be smoking before each batch. Crowding drops the temperature and creates steam. |
| Mise takes forever — 45+ minutes of prep | New to this workflow; everything feels slow | That's normal for Session 4. The point isn't speed — it's learning to work linearly instead of chaotically. Prep time shortens dramatically once the habit is built. By Session 20 this will be unconscious. |

**Drill: The Stir-Fry Countdown**

Before you start cooking: line up every component in the order it goes into the pan. Beef first (in small batches), then aromatics, then broccoli, then sauce. Point at each one and say the order out loud. Sounds silly. Works. In a stir-fry, the difference between success and a soggy mess is sequencing, not technique.

**Instructor's Insight:**

> Mise en place is not "do all the prep first." It's "think through the entire cook before you start." There's a difference. The prep part is mechanical — you can teach anyone to chop an onion. The thinking part is what separates home cooks who follow recipes from home cooks who *run their kitchen*. Tonight, before you prep, take 2 minutes and read the recipe start to finish. Picture each step. That mental walkthrough is the real mise en place — the cutting and measuring just execute the plan you've already made.

### ⏰ Service 1 — French Onion Soup

**What Success Looks Like:**

- Your onion half-moons are thin and consistent — close to ⅛" thick, and no slice is obviously thicker than its neighbors.
- The onions caramelize evenly. No burned patches next to pale, undercooked sections. If your slices were uniform, this happens almost automatically.
- The finished soup has deep, sweet, complex onion flavor — not raw-onion bite, not burned-sugar bitterness. It should taste like it took three hours (because it did).

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Slices are wildly inconsistent in thickness | Not maintaining consistent downward pressure through the whole stroke | Go slower. Let the weight of the knife do the work. You're guiding, not forcing. Each stroke should look and feel the same as the last. |
| Onions burn in spots | The thick slices release more water and the thin slices sit dry against the pan bottom | This is the lesson. Uniform thickness prevents exactly this. Also: stir more frequently in the first 15 minutes, and keep the heat at medium or just below. |
| The whole cook takes 5+ hours | It's a slow recipe and you underestimated the cutting time | This is a weekend Service meal for a reason. The slicing alone takes 20–30 minutes, and caramelization is 45–90 minutes. Budget accordingly. It's worth it. |

**Instructor's Insight:**

> French onion soup is the most honest dish you'll cook in this program. There are exactly three skills that determine whether it's great: knife consistency, heat control, and patience. There is no sauce to hide behind, no spice blend to compensate. If your slices are uneven, you'll taste it. If you rushed the caramelization, you'll taste it. This is why it's the first Service meal.

## Block 2 — Named Cuts & Precision

---

### Session 5 — The Julienne

**What Success Looks Like:**

- Your matchsticks are roughly 2–3 inches long and ⅛" wide. They don't need to be perfect — but 80% of them should be close enough that they'd cook at the same rate in a hot pan.
- You squared off the vegetable first. If your carrot still has a curved face when you start slicing planks, your matchsticks will come out wedge-shaped instead of rectangular.
- Your chiffonade basil is in clean ribbons, not bruised mush. The leaves should look like delicate green confetti, not wilted paste.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Vegetable rolls while you're cutting | Didn't create a flat face first | Before anything else: cut a thin slice off one side of the carrot or zucchini to create a flat, stable base. Set it flat-side down. It shouldn't move. |
| Matchsticks are wedge-shaped — thick on one side, thin on the other | Planks weren't uniform thickness | Cut planks first, then stack 2–3 at a time and cut across. If the planks are uneven, the matchsticks will be too. Fix the planks. |
| Basil turns black within minutes | Knife is tearing, not cutting | Hone. Stack the leaves tightly, roll into a cigar, and cut with a single clean stroke per ribbon — don't saw back and forth. |

**Drill: The Ruler Test**

After you julienne your carrots, line 10 matchsticks up side by side on the cutting board. They should look like a set, not a grab bag. If most are within ⅛" of each other, you're doing it right. If there are outliers twice the width of the others, slow down and focus on plank thickness next time. This takes 5 seconds and gives you immediate visual feedback.

**Instructor's Insight:**

> Listen to your knife. A sharp blade through a carrot makes a clean, quiet snap. If you hear tearing or the carrot cracks and splits ahead of the blade, your knife is dull or your angle is wrong. Sound is one of the fastest feedback loops you have — faster than looking, because you hear every cut in real time. Once you start listening, you'll catch problems before you see them.

### Session 6 — The Dice (Small, Medium, Large)

**What Success Looks Like:**

- You understand the sequence: slab → stick → cube. This is how every professional dice works, at every size. If you skip a step, the cubes come out uneven.
- Your ½" dice for the home fries is consistent enough that the potatoes brown at roughly the same rate in the pan. No tiny burned bits next to pale undercooked chunks.
- You remember what the three-size experiment showed you. The small dice finished first. The large dice was still raw. **That's the lesson:** cut size controls cooking time.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Cubes come out as rectangles or trapezoids | Batons weren't square; planks weren't consistent | Go back one step. If the cubes are wrong, the batons were wrong. If the batons were wrong, the planks were wrong. Fix the first cut and everything downstream improves. |
| Potatoes oxidize and turn gray before you cook them | Took too long between cutting and cooking | Keep a bowl of cold water nearby. Drop diced potatoes in as you cut. Drain and pat completely dry before the pan — wet potatoes steam instead of sear. |
| The dice experiment feels wasteful | Three separate potatoes diced at three sizes seems like wasted food | Use one potato for the experiment. Cut it into three sections: dice one section small, one medium, one large. Cook the experiment in a couple tablespoons of oil in a small pan. Then dice the rest of your potatoes at ½" for the home fries. One potato, one lesson — the rest become dinner. |

**Instructor's Insight:**

> Professional cooks don't dice fast because they move their hands fast. They dice fast because they never make a cut that doesn't produce the shape they want. Every stroke counts. Home cooks make 30 cuts and get 20 usable pieces. Professionals make 20 cuts and get 20 usable pieces. The speed difference isn't hand speed — it's waste elimination. When you practice, focus on making every cut intentional. Speed follows precision, not the other way around.

### Session 7 — Breaking Down a Whole Chicken

**What Success Looks Like:**

- You get 10 pieces: 2 breasts, 2 thighs, 2 drumsticks, 2 wings, and a 2-piece carcass (backbone and rib section). If you're getting 8, you didn't separate the drumsticks from the thighs.
- The joints separated without you having to force the knife through bone. If you hit bone, you missed the joint — stop, wiggle the piece, find the seam, and try again.
- The skin on each piece is mostly intact, not shredded. Clean cuts through connective tissue, not sawing through skin.
- The carcass is in a zip-lock bag in the freezer. This is non-negotiable — it's your Block 7 stock.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Cutting through bone instead of joints | Can't find the joint by feel | Put the knife down for a second. Grab the leg and move it — bend, twist, rotate. Watch where the skin creases and where the bone pivots. That pivot point is the joint. Cut there. The knife should slide through with almost no resistance. |
| Shredding the skin | Sawing back and forth with too much pressure | Use the tip of the knife for precision cuts (removing backbone, separating thigh from drumstick) and long, smooth strokes with the heel for the larger separations (leg from body). Don't saw. |
| Pieces are uneven — one breast is much larger than the other | The cut along the breastbone wasn't centered | Follow the keel bone (the ridge running down the center of the breast). Keep your blade right against it and let the bone guide you. If you drift, one breast gets the lion's share. |
| Breakdown takes 40+ minutes | Totally normal the first time | It will. Watch the video before you start, not during. Try to do it in under 20 on your second chicken (Block 7–8 area). Under 10 is a realistic goal by the end of the program. |

**Drill: The Joint Test**

Before you make any cut, locate all four major joints by hand: the two hip joints (where legs meet body) and the two shoulder joints (where wings meet body). Wiggle each one. Feel the pivot. Point the tip of the knife at each joint. Now start. If you can find the joints by touch, you can break down any bird — chicken, turkey, duck — because the anatomy is the same.

**Instructor's Insight:**

> A whole chicken costs less per pound than any individual cut, and you get better quality meat because it hasn't been sitting in a tray losing moisture. But the real reason you learn this here is control. Once you can break down a whole chicken by feel — working with the anatomy instead of against it — your relationship with every protein changes. You start seeing joints, seams, and grain direction in pork shoulders and beef roasts too. This isn't a chicken skill. It's a butchery foundation.

### Session 8 — Speed Benchmark

**What Success Looks Like:**

- You have a number written down: how long it took to dice one onion. That's the only metric that matters tonight.
- The number is probably between 3 and 7 minutes. Professional line cooks do it in 30–60 seconds. You are not a professional line cook — yet. The gap between your number and theirs is the entire rest of this program.
- Your dice is consistent enough that the shakshuka cooks evenly — no raw onion chunks in a sea of dissolved onion.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Cutting through the root end and the onion falls apart | Didn't leave the root intact | The root holds the onion together while you make horizontal and vertical cuts. Cut close to it but never through it. Remove it at the very end. |
| Onion slips on the board | Board is wet, or onion is sitting on its curved side | Place cut-side down after halving through the root. Dry the board if it's wet. A damp paper towel under the board keeps the board from sliding on the counter. |
| You forget to write down the time | It doesn't seem important in the moment | It is. This is the only quantitative measure in the first four weeks. You'll time yourself again at the end of Block 4 and the improvement will surprise you. Write it on the recipe page, on your phone, on your hand — somewhere it sticks. |

**Instructor's Insight:**

> Speed is a byproduct, never a goal. If you chase speed, you'll develop sloppy habits that you'll spend months unlearning. If you chase precision and consistency, speed shows up on its own — because you stop making wasted movements and corrective cuts. The benchmark isn't here to pressure you. It's here to prove to yourself, two blocks from now, that deliberate practice works.

### ⏰ Service 2 — Provençal Vegetable Tian

**What Success Looks Like:**

- Your slices are ⅛"–¼" thick and uniform enough that when you shingle them in the dish, they create a clean, repeating pattern. This dish is visually dramatic — and the visual drama is a direct function of slice consistency.
- Every vegetable (eggplant, zucchini, squash, tomato) is cut to the same target thickness. Not "close enough" — the same. Different vegetables at different thicknesses cook at different rates and the dish falls apart.
- The finished tian looks like something a restaurant would serve. That's the standard for a Service meal.

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Why it happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Slices start thick and get thinner as you go | Your hand position drifts as the vegetable gets smaller | Slow down toward the end of each vegetable. When it gets short, your natural tendency is to speed up and take bigger bites. Resist. |
| Eggplant slices brown and oxidize before assembly | Eggplant oxidizes fast once cut | Slice it last, right before assembly. Or brush slices lightly with olive oil as you go. |
| The tian looks good but tastes bland | Not enough seasoning between layers | Season every layer — salt, pepper, a drizzle of olive oil, fresh herbs between the shingles. The tian is a stack of individual slices, and each one needs to be seasoned as if it were standing alone. |

**Instructor's Insight:**

> The tian is your first glimpse of a principle that runs through the entire program: technique is flavor. The same vegetables, sliced thick and tossed in a roasting pan, would make a decent side dish. Sliced thin and uniform, layered deliberately, seasoned between layers — they become something entirely different. You didn't add different ingredients. You just applied more control. That gap between "decent" and "stunning" shows up in every block from here forward.

## Equipment Confidence Check

Do this before Session 1, and again at the start of Block 2.

**The Tomato Test:** Set a ripe tomato on the cutting board. Rest the blade on the skin (don't press down) and draw it gently across the surface. If the knife catches the skin and slices in under its own weight, you're good. If it slides across the surface without biting, hone it before you start — or it needs professional sharpening. A dull knife is more dangerous than a sharp one because it forces you to push harder, which is exactly when you lose control.

**The Paper Test:** Hold a single sheet of paper at the top edge. Draw the blade downward through it. A sharp knife cuts cleanly. A dull knife catches, tears, or won't cut at all.

If either test fails: hone the knife with 5–6 strokes per side on a honing steel. Re-test. If it still fails, the edge is too far gone for honing — it needs to be sharpened. Most kitchen stores will do this for $5–10.

## What You're Building Toward

These two weeks aren't really about knife skills. They're about building the feedback loop that makes you better at everything that comes after.

By the end of Block 2, you should have:

- [ ] A grip that's becoming automatic (you stop having to think about it)
- [ ] The ability to look at a pile of diced vegetables and notice when it's uneven
- [ ] A feel for the difference between rocking and pushing through a cut
- [ ] One timed benchmark to compare against later
- [ ] A whole chicken broken down and none of it wasted
- [ ] A freezer bag with a carcass for Block 7 stock
- [ ] 10 dinners cooked — every single session produced a real meal

That last one is the most important. You didn't do drills. You cooked dinner. The practice was built into the meal. This is how every block in the program works.
