# 📋 Curriculum Review — Block 1–2: Knife Skills & Kitchen Setup

> **Purpose:** Specific, actionable recommendations for improving the Block 1–2 lesson. Organized by priority. Each item includes what to change, where, and why.

---

## What's Working (Don't Touch)

These are the structural strengths of the current lesson. Any revision should preserve them:

1. **Every session produces a real meal.** This is the single best design decision in the curriculum. It respects the student's time, eliminates "drill fatigue," and means every skill is immediately applied in context. Protect this at all costs.

2. **The Skill → Meal → Table → Compare Notes pattern.** Consistent, scannable, and it works as both a reading document and an in-kitchen reference. Don't break the rhythm.

3. **The progression is sound.** Grip → claw → cuts → mise en place → named cuts → breakdown → benchmark. Each session builds on the previous one. The sequencing is tight.

4. **The voice is excellent.** Direct, opinionated, second-person. Reads like a knowledgeable friend who happens to be a chef instructor. Doesn't condescend. Doesn't hedge. "Non-negotiable." "That's the entire lesson." — this is the right register for serious home cooks.

5. **The video curation is strong.** "Compare Notes" gives students a second perspective without undermining the lesson's authority. Smart framing.

## Priority 1 — Fix Now (High Impact, Easy)

### 1.1 — Add a warm-up drill to Session 1

**Problem:** Session 1 is the only session with no deliberate practice before cooking. The student goes straight from reading about the pinch grip to cooking pasta. Every other session (2, 3, 5, 6) has a pre-cook drill. Session 1 should too.

**Fix:** Add a 2-minute drill after the Skill paragraph — mince one garlic clove and a few parsley sprigs using the pinch grip. The minced garlic goes into the pasta. No wasted food, no wasted time.

**Where in index.md:** After "Hold it this way for the entire meal." (~line 21), before "**Full Meal:**"

---

### 1.2 — Fix the three-potato experiment in Session 6

**Problem:** The feedback log says: *"Session 7 the potato experiment is wasteful and I didn't do it. Watching the video from Kenji is sufficient IMO."* They're right — dicing three whole potatoes at three sizes only to demonstrate a principle is wasteful for a home cook.

**Fix:** Rework the drill: use one potato cut into three sections — dice one section small, one medium, one large. Cook the experiment in a small pan. Then dice the rest uniformly for the home fries. One potato for the lesson, the rest for dinner.

**Where in index.md:** Replace the existing skill paragraph in Session 6 (~lines 139–143). Keep the punchline ("That's the entire lesson.") — it's great. Just change the setup so it's one potato, not three.

## Priority 2 — Add to the Lesson (High Impact, Moderate Effort)

### 2.1 — Add "What Success Looks Like" to both Service meals

**Problem:** Students don't know what "good enough" looks like. The Service meals have `*Skill targets:*` — but those are terse checklists, not success descriptions. A student cooking French Onion Soup for the first time needs to know: what does a correctly caramelized onion base look like? What does "uniform half-moons" actually mean in terms of thickness and appearance?

**Fix:** Add 3–5 bullet "What Success Looks Like" paragraphs after each Service meal's Compare Notes section.

**Template:**

> **✅ What Success Looks Like**
>
> - Your half-moons are within ⅛" of each other — consistent enough that they caramelize at the same rate
> - No burned patches next to raw sections — uniform thickness prevents exactly this
> - The finished soup tastes deeply sweet, not sharp or bitter

**Where in index.md:** After Service 1 Compare Notes (~line 109) and after Service 2 Compare Notes (~line 207), before the next H2 section.

**Note:** Consider extending this pattern to every session if the pilot works. Start with the Service meals — they're the highest-stakes moments.

---

### 2.2 — Add "Common Mistakes" to each block

**Problem:** The lesson tells students what to do, but rarely warns them what goes wrong and why. Mistakes in knife skills are predictable — every instructor has seen the same five things a thousand times. Naming them in advance saves students frustration and injuries.

**Fix:** Add a short (3–5 bullet) "Common Mistakes" section after each block's Service meal, covering the most frequent errors from the entire block.

**Block 1 examples:**
- Reverting to handle grip without noticing
- Lifting the claw hand away from the food
- Using only part of the blade
- Skipping the hone

**Block 2 examples:**
- Skipping the flat-face cut before julienning
- Forcing the knife through chicken bone instead of finding the joint
- Rushing straight to cubes without establishing planks first

**Where in index.md:** After the new "What Success Looks Like" sections recommended in 2.1.

---

### 2.3 — Add 2 instructor-style insights to build intuition

**Problem:** The lesson explains techniques well but rarely gives the "pro insight" that changes how students think. Two specific opportunities:

**Insight 1 — Session 2 (The Claw):** Add after the skill paragraph:

> *Your guide hand does more work than your knife hand. The knife follows the knuckles — if the knuckles move at a consistent pace, the cuts come out even. Stop watching the blade. Start watching the hand feeding the food.*

This reframes the claw from "a safety technique" to "the hand that determines cut quality." That's a significant conceptual shift.

**Insight 2 — Session 5 (The Julienne):** Add after the chiffonade paragraph:

> *Listen to your knife. A sharp blade through a carrot makes a clean, quiet snap. If you hear tearing or the carrot cracks ahead of the blade, your knife is dull or your angle is wrong. Sound is one of the fastest feedback loops you have.*

This introduces sensory feedback beyond visual. Most knife-skills instruction is entirely visual. Sound is faster.

---

### 2.4 — Add an equipment confidence check

**Problem:** The "Before You Start" section tells students to hone before every session, but never teaches them how to know if their knife is actually sharp enough. A student whose knife is dull from day one will have a frustrating and potentially dangerous Block 1.

**Fix:** Add a "Quick check" after the honing rule:

> **Quick check:** Set a ripe tomato on the board. Rest the blade on the skin and draw it across without pressing. If the knife catches the skin and slices in under its own weight, you're good. If it slides across, hone it — or it needs professional sharpening.

**Where in index.md:** After "**Rule for the week:** Hone your knife before every session. Non-negotiable." (~line 14)

## Priority 3 — Polish (Lower Impact, Worth Doing)

### 3.1 — Add Skill targets to every session, not just Service meals

**Problem:** The `*Skill targets:*` italic checklist only appears on Service meals. Regular sessions would benefit from a similar one-liner at the end of the component table. For example:

- Session 1: *Skill targets: Pinch grip maintained throughout. No handle-grip reversion.*
- Session 4: *Skill targets: Every ingredient prepped and staged before the burner goes on. Zero mid-cook scrambling.*

This gives each session a testable endpoint.

---

### 3.2 — Strengthen the "Buy for Block 2" note in shopping-list.md

**Problem:** The whole chicken note in the shopping list says to buy it "the day before you plan to break it down." This is important enough to be bolder — it's the only time-sensitive purchase in the block, and forgetting it kills the session.

**Fix:** Add a sentence: "If you forget the whole chicken, this session doesn't work. Put it on a calendar reminder for the day before."

**Status:** This line already exists (~line 115). It's good. No change needed.

## Priority 4 — Consider for Later (Structural Changes)

### 4.1 — Rethink the feedback template

**Problem:** The feedback file has 25+ prompts and most are blank. The student filled in 4 out of 25. Open-ended prompts aren't generating engagement.

**Possible fix:** Replace open-ended prompts with shorter, more specific ones:
- "Rate your onion dice consistency 1–5"
- "Did you hone before every session? Y/N"
- "Name one thing that clicked"
- "Name one thing you'd change"

This is a structural change that affects all blocks — flag it for a program-wide revision, not a Block 1–2 specific fix.

---

### 4.2 — Session 2 recipe length

**Problem:** Feedback: "Chicken Noodle Soup was a bit long." The feedback log also notes a possible recipe swap.

**Recommendation:** Keep the recipe for now — it's pedagogically sound (the mirepoix prep is the lesson). But flag it as a candidate for swap if a shorter recipe emerges that still requires dicing carrots, celery, and onion as the primary prep step.

---

### 4.3 — Consider a "Block Summary" card

A single-page, printable card with:
- All 10 sessions listed with skill + dish
- Equipment needed
- The 3 most important rules for the block
- Space for the timed benchmark

This would serve the student who wants a physical artifact on the kitchen counter. Low priority relative to content improvements, but a nice-to-have.
