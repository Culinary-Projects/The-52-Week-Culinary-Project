# Curriculum Restructuring Recommendations
## From 62 Weeks → 52 Weeks Without Losing the Good Stuff

**Date:** March 2026  
**Status:** Recommendations only — no changes have been made

---

## Terminology

Going forward the hierarchy is: **Unit → Block → Session**, with cumulative session numbering maintained across the entire program (Sessions never reset between blocks or units). Labs and Services also keep their cumulative counters.

---

## The Problem in Plain Terms

The program currently has **29 blocks covering 58 weeks of content**, plus a **4-week gap** at weeks 49–52 that was left in by accident. The intended program was 62 weeks. The target is 52 weeks.

To land at exactly 52 weeks, the math is:
- **Cut 8 weeks** from existing content (4 block merges): saves 8 weeks
- **Rebrand 1 pairing** (Middle Eastern + SE Asian → "Spice Road"): saves 0 weeks, stays as 2 separate 2-week blocks
- **Add 1 new Poultry block** inside Unit 3 (The Proteins): costs 2 weeks
- **Distribute 3 homeless topics** (cold soups, quick breads, frozen desserts) as session additions into existing blocks: costs 0 weeks
- Net result: 58 − 8 + 2 = **52 weeks, 26 blocks**

This is not about making the program easier or shorter. It's about making it tighter. Everything that matters stays. What gets cut is redundancy, not depth.

---

## The Philosophy Check

Before the cuts, a reminder of what this program is. The home chef it's building should be able to:

- Cook anything in their weekly rotation without looking anything up
- Tackle a 3-course dinner for 8 people with confidence
- Understand *why* techniques work, not just how to follow recipes
- Spend their cooking time making food they actually want to eat

That last point is the hard constraint throughout. Every session has to be a real meal, not a drill. A session on cold soup is also dinner. A session on biscuits is also breakfast. This doesn't change.

## The Four Cuts (+ One Rebrand)

### Cut 1: Midpoint Review Block → Eliminated
**Saves: 2 weeks**

The midpoint review block exists to benchmark where the student is. That instinct is correct — accountability checkpoints matter. But a full 2-week block for pure review is too much. The cook-from-memory drill, the knife benchmark, and the skills inventory can all be embedded as the final two sessions of Unit 2 Block 7 (Pasta & Grains) before crossing into Unit 3. Nothing is lost except the dedicated block wrapper around it.

**What to preserve:** Bake the knife speed benchmark and the "cook from memory" session into the closing sessions of Block 7.

---

### Cut 2: Two Fish Blocks → One Fish Block
**Saves: 2 weeks**

We currently give fish **four weeks** — the same total time as beef, and more than any individual world cuisine. *The Professional Chef* covers fish in a single chapter. Four weeks for a home cook is a lot, especially since many sessions across the two fish blocks cover similar techniques (pan-sear, en papillote, poaching) applied to different species.

**What to preserve:** Build a single tight 2-week block: Lab (fillet a round fish), Lab (fillet a flat fish), pan-seared crispy skin, whole roasted fish, poached fish, en papillote, and the full fish dinner Service. That's already 7 sessions and a Service — plenty.

**What gets cut:** The second fish block's species deep-dives (sole, skate, bass in full) become variations within surviving sessions rather than standalone sessions.

**Gravlax duplication fix:** Gravlax currently appears as a full stand-alone session in both the Fish block (Session 90) and the Charcuterie block (Session 114), including the tasting/board reveal session in each. This is a straight duplicate. **Remove gravlax from the Fish block entirely.** It belongs in Pork & Charcuterie, which is where the curing discipline lives. The Fish block doesn't need it — it already has enough material without it, and the cure logic fits far better next to pancetta and sausage making.

---

### Cut 3: Pork Block + Charcuterie Review Block → One Block (Pork & Charcuterie)
**Saves: 2 weeks**

These two blocks are the same subject. Pork is the source protein for charcuterie; the topics are inseparable. The current structure creates an artificial seam where the student stops cooking pork, then immediately starts a "pork but aged" block. Merge them into a single unified 2-week block.

**What to preserve:** Sausage making, gravlax (moved here from Fish — see above), pancetta/cured belly, chicken liver pâté, quick pickles, and the full charcuterie board Service all survive within this block.

---

### Cut 4: Signature Dishes Block + Menus & Timing Block → One Block
**Saves: 2 weeks**

These two blocks serve consecutive, closely related purposes: build something original, then learn to orchestrate it at scale. A single 2-week block that starts with signature dish development and closes with full menu planning and the dinner party execution is tighter and more logical.

**What to preserve:** Both Services (the dinner party and the personal tasting menu) survive. Scaling recipes and mise en place at scale are retained as sessions.

---

### Spice Road: Middle Eastern + SE Asian → Shared Theme, Still 2 Blocks
**Saves: 0 weeks — this is a rebrand, not a merge**

Middle Eastern (weeks 41–42) and SE Asian (weeks 43–44) stay as two separate 2-week blocks. Both Services survive unchanged. The "Spice Road" framing is a narrative choice: it leans into cross-cultural technique connections (fermented pastes, dried chiles, tamarind, fish sauce) rather than treating the two blocks as completely unrelated. No content is cut from either block.

---

## The New Block: Poultry (Inside Unit 3, The Proteins)

Poultry goes inside Unit 3 — **not** at the end of the program. It sits logically between Shellfish and Pork & Charcuterie, completing the protein survey before the program moves into world cuisines.

The program currently has no dedicated poultry block. Chicken appears scattered across multiple units. Duck is handled as a tack-on inside the Lamb block and again inside French. Turkey doesn't appear at all. There's no moment where the student sits down and thinks systematically about birds.

**This block covers 8 sessions across 2 weeks:**

| Session | Dish | Technique |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Spatchcocked roast chicken | Breaking down oven heat; fat basting vs. dry skin |
| 2 | Poached whole chicken + pan sauce from the stock | Most underrated home cook technique; nothing wasted |
| 3 | Pan-seared chicken breast + thigh comparison | Direct heat; why thighs forgive and breasts don't |
| 4 | Braised chicken thighs (coq au vin adjacent) | Braising poultry; liquid reduction; built-in sauce |
| 5 | Turkey breast roast + thigh braise (same day) | The home cook's real turkey challenge; no whole bird required |
| 6 | Duck leg confit | Low-temperature fat cooking; texture vs. time |
| 7 | Pan-seared duck breast | The reverse method; scoring fat; resting rules |
| **Service** | The Whole Bird Dinner | Roast chicken + duck breast; two birds, one dinner |

**What's not in this block:** Chicken liver pâté stays in Pork & Charcuterie (it's a curing/preservation session, not a poultry-technique session). The "breaking down a whole chicken" Lab from Block 1 is not repeated — that skill is already established.

**Meal-first:** Every session is dinner. Poached chicken is dinner. A turkey breast roast is dinner. Duck confit is one of the best things you can eat on a Tuesday.

## Three Gaps Resolved by Adding Sessions to Existing Blocks

These don't need new blocks. Each topic fits naturally inside an existing block where it conceptually belongs.

### Cold Soups → Add 2 sessions to Unit 1, Block 4 (Stocks & Eggs)

Cold soups are just soup technique with different finishing logic — more acid, more salt at cold temperature, blender texture control. This is a direct extension of what Block 4 already teaches about stock, soup architecture, and building flavor in liquid.

- **Add: Vichyssoise** — cold leek and potato; teaches finishing seasoning at temperature, fat-forward texture. Dinner opener.
- **Add: Gazpacho** — blender acid soup; teaches balance of acid, oil, and seasoning in a cold state. Summer dinner.

Replace or compress one of the repetitive warm soup sessions in Block 4 to make room. The block already covers chicken stock, fish stock, vegetable stock, risotto, and several soups — it can absorb two cold sessions without expanding.

### Quick Breads → Add 2 sessions to Unit 5, Block (Bread)

Biscuits, scones, and cornbread are baking. They belong next to sourdough and brioche — not anywhere near pad thai. The Bread block currently has two no-knead variation sessions that cover largely similar ground (basic fold technique applied twice). Replace one of those and redirect the freed slot.

- **Add: Buttermilk Biscuits** — teaches fat lamination (coating flour in fat before adding liquid), quick chemical leavening, hot oven. Dinner is biscuits and gravy or biscuits with jam and butter.
- **Add: Cornbread / Scones** — skillet cornbread is one of the most practical quick breads for a home cook; pairs with everything. If the student prefers a sweet breakfast, scones are the same technique family.

Also add the **baguette** as a third session here, replacing the second no-knead variation. Lean dough, scoring, steam in the first minutes of baking. One of the most useful bakes the student will never have made before. The current Bread block already covers sourdough, focaccia, brioche, and croissants — a baguette round out the lean/enriched spectrum properly.

### Frozen Desserts → Add 2 sessions to Unit 5, Block (Pastry)

No-churn ice cream and sorbet sit naturally after custards (crème brûlée, panna cotta, chocolate mousse). The Pastry block teaches the custard base; frozen desserts are the logical cold extension.

- **Add: No-Churn Ice Cream** — condensed milk + whipped cream base; teaches the structure of a frozen emulsion without a machine. Real dessert, any night.
- **Add: Sorbet + Granita** — sugar syrup + fruit; granita teaches manual agitation intervals; sorbet teaches balance of sugar to water for texture. No machine required.

Also add one **chocolate tempering + ganache** session here — the minimum home cook version of chocolate work. Teaches crystalline structure, bloom, and how to make truffles and ganache-based fillings that don't seize.

## Gaps That Don't Need Fixing

| Gap | Decision | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Consommé / clarification** | Skip | Once-in-a-lifetime technique for a home cook. Time cost vs. frequency of use doesn't justify a session. |
| **Veal stock** | Skip | We substitute chicken/beef stock throughout. Veal is expensive, harder to source, and the flavor difference is marginal outside a restaurant context. |
| **Forcemeat / galantines / aspic** | Skip | Catering-grade garde manger. Not a home cook skill. |
| **Cake and icing (full chapter)** | Keep Service as-is | One well-executed layer cake Service is the right depth for a home cook who isn't becoming a pastry specialist. |
| **Turkey (systematic)** | Addressed in new Poultry block | See above. |
| **Nutrition** | Skip | Not what this program is. |
| **Hors d'oeuvres / canapés** | Skip | The Service dinners teach this implicitly. A dedicated chapter would be out of character. |

## What Doesn't Change

- **Unit → Block → Session** terminology throughout
- **Cumulative session numbering** — Sessions, Services, and Labs keep their global counters; the numbers will shift as blocks are renumbered, handled during implementation
- **Lab sessions** — all 9 Labs survive untouched; they're opt-in depth
- **Free resources only** — no session depends on a paid subscription or book
- **Meal-first principle** — every session produces real food; no session is a drill without dinner
