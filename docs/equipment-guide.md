# Kitchen Equipment Guide

> **For students starting out:** This guide lists every piece of equipment used across all 26 blocks of the program, when you'll first need it, and how essential it is. Use this to plan your purchases in phases — one module at a time — rather than buying everything upfront.

**Three tiers:**
- 🔴 **Essential** — You cannot complete the skill without this. Buy before the block shown.
- 🟡 **Recommended** — You'll use it repeatedly and improvisation is awkward. Worth buying.
- 🟢 **Optional** — Nice to have. A workaround exists and is noted.

---

## How to Use This Guide

The first module (Blocks 1–8) requires around 14 essential items. That's not a short or cheap list — expect $150–400 depending on what you already own. The upside is that almost everything from Module 1 is used in every subsequent module. You're building a kitchen, not buying a kit for one class.

**Buy in phases:**
1. **Before Module 1:** Everything in the Module 1 section below. Get this sorted before Block 1.
2. **Before each subsequent module:** Check the module's section and buy new Essential items at least a week before the block where they first appear.
3. **World's Kitchens equipment:** Buy in sub-batches as each cuisine arrives — see the Module 4 section for the breakdown by block.

## Buying by Module

### Before Module 1 (Blocks 1–8): Foundational Skills — ~$150–400

Everything here is used constantly from Block 1 onward.

**Essential**
- Chef's knife, 8–10 inch (German or Japanese)
- Cutting board, large plastic or wood (at least 12×18 in)
- Honing steel
- Kitchen shears / scissors (keep two pairs)
- Tongs, 12-inch locking (×2)
- Instant-read thermometer
- Half-sheet pans (×2)
- 10-inch stainless steel or carbon steel skillet
- 2-qt saucepan
- 8–12 qt stockpot
- Fine-mesh strainer
- Wooden spoons (×2)
- Silicone spatula
- Ladle

**Recommended**
- Paring knife
- Box grater
- Food processor
- Microplane / rasp grater

---

### Before Module 2 (Blocks 9–16): Building Flavor — ~$100–250

These items first appear mid-module — you don't need them all on Day 1 of Block 9.

**Needed by Block 13**
- Potato ricer — gnocchi and smooth mash; *workaround: hand masher (less smooth)*
- Pasta machine, hand-cranked — fresh pasta; *workaround: rolling pin + patience*

**Needed by Block 15**
- 12-inch cast iron skillet — non-negotiable for beef sear and oven-to-stove cooking
- Dutch oven, 5–7 qt (enameled cast iron) — workhorse across modules 2–5
- Roasting pan with rack — also used for bain-marie
- Wire rack (fits half-sheet pan) — resting meat, draining

---

### Before Module 3 (Blocks 17–28): The Proteins — ~$20–70

Smaller additions; the cast iron investment happened at the end of Module 2.

**Essential**
- Fish spatula, thin flexible slotted (first needed Block 19)
- Kitchen twine — trussing roasts and poultry (first needed Block 21)
- Spider strainer / skimmer — shellfish, blanching (first needed Block 23)

**Recommended**
- Shellfish brush — cleaning mussels and clams (first needed Block 23)

---

### Before Module 4 (Blocks 29–44): The World's Kitchens — ~$100–250

Buy in sub-batches as each cuisine arrives — no point buying a wok at Block 29.

**Before Block 33 (Japanese)** — ~$30–50
- Tamagoyaki pan (rectangular) — *workaround: small nonstick pan*
- Steamer insert or tamale pot — dumplings, tamales, vegetables
- Bamboo skewers — yakitori, satay (optional)

**Before Block 35 (Chinese)** — ~$50–80
- Wok, 14-inch carbon steel — round or flat-bottom depending on burner
- High-powered blender — mole, curry paste, smooth soups

**Before Block 37 (Mexican)** — ~$30–50
- Comal or flat cast iron griddle — tortillas, flatbreads
- Tortilla press, cast iron or aluminum — *workaround: two heavy skillet bottoms + parchment paper*

**Before Block 43 (Southeast Asian)** — ~$30–60
- Mortar and pestle — Thai curry pastes; *workaround: food processor (inferior result)*
- Pressure cooker / Instant Pot — braising acceleration; *workaround: Dutch oven + 3× the time* (optional)

---

### Before Module 5 (Blocks 45–52): Mastery & Pastry — ~$200–400

The biggest single-module equipment investment. Spread the cost across Module 4 if needed — most items can be sourced during Blocks 41–44.

**Essential**
- Stand mixer with dough hook, whisk, and paddle — *workaround for bread: by hand. For pastry: hand mixer for most tasks.*
- Digital kitchen scale (0.1 g resolution) — required for bread; also recommended from Block 1 for all baking
- Bench scraper (metal)
- Banneton / proofing basket — *workaround: bowl lined with floured cloth*
- Bread lame (scoring blade) — *workaround: very sharp serrated knife or razor blade*
- 9-inch tart pan with removable bottom
- 9-inch pie dish
- 9-inch round cake pans (×3) — for layer cakes
- Ramekins, 4–6 oz (set of 6) — crème brûlée, soufflé, bain-marie desserts
- Kitchen torch + butane canister — crème brûlée, browning meringue
- Candy thermometer (or second instant-read) — sugar work, deep frying
- Whetstone, 1000/3000 grit — knife sharpening

**Recommended**
- Pullman loaf pan with lid — sandwich bread; *workaround: standard loaf pan*
- Pastry brush — egg wash, glazing, greasing
- Pastry bag + tips (set) — *workaround: ziplock bag with corner snipped*
- Pie weights (or dried beans) — blind-baking pastry shells
- Multiple-channel timer — managing multi-dish service; *workaround: phone timers*

**Optional**
- Springform pan, 9-inch — *workaround: regular cake pan with parchment*
- Madeleine pan
- Mandoline slicer

## Notes on Quality

- **Knife:** One great knife beats a block of mediocre ones. If you're buying new, the [Victorinox Fibrox 8-inch](https://www.victorinox.com) gives professional results at home-cook prices. Japanese gyuto knives offer better edge retention but require more maintenance.
- **Cast iron:** Lodge skillet and Dutch oven are the standard; pre-seasoned and durable for decades.
- **Thermometer:** ThermoWorks Thermapen or ThermoPop are the reliable standards. Harbor Freight / cheap probes read slow and drift.
- **Scale:** OXO Good Grips, Escali, or My Weigh KD-8000 all hit the 0.1g sweet spot.
- **Dutch oven:** Le Creuset and Staub are lifetime purchases. Lodge enamel is the budget-friendly alternative.
