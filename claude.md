# 🍳 Home Culinary School: Weeks 1–8

### A 52-Week Program for the Home Cook Who Already Cooks

-----

> **Your Schedule:** Monday · Tuesday · Wednesday · Friday cook | **Saturday** = Big Project | **Thursday & Sunday** = Off
> 
> **Philosophy:** Every session layers a professional skill into a meal you’d be cooking anyway. Skills compound. Confidence grows.

-----

## Weekly Structure

Each week follows the same rhythm:

|Element              |What it is                                                                                            |
|---------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**The Skill**        |One technique practiced with intention                                                                |
|**The Meal**         |A complete dinner built around that skill — protein + veg + starch (or salad)                         |
|**The Read**         |A short article that explains the *why* behind what you cooked                                        |
|**Saturday Project** |A bigger cook that makes the week’s skills visible                                                    |
|**Sunday Reflection**|3 sentences in your notes app. The habit that separates cooks who plateau from cooks who keep growing.|

# 🔪 WEEKS 1–2: Knife Skills & Kitchen Setup

> *“Learn to hold a knife properly and half the battle is won.”*

-----

## Week 1 — Your Knife Is a Liar (And Your Hands Don’t Know It Yet)

**Before you cook anything this week**, read:

- 📖 [How to Hold a Chef’s Knife](https://www.seriouseats.com/knife-skills-how-to-hold-a-knife) — the pinch grip vs. handle grip, with photos
- 📖 [The 4 Knife Cuts Every Cook Should Know](https://www.seriouseats.com/knife-skills-the-4-knife-cuts-every-cook-should-know) — the slice, chop, back-slice, and rock chop

**Rule for the week:** Hone your knife before every session. Non-negotiable.

-----

### Monday — The Pinch Grip

**Skill:** Switch to the blade pinch grip tonight and don’t revert. Your thumb and forefinger rest directly on the blade just in front of the bolster — it’s intimidating, but it gives you far better control. Hold it this way for the entire meal.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Pasta with Butter and Parmesan](https://www.seriouseats.com/pasta-with-butter-and-parmesan-recipe)

The prep is simple — garlic and parsley — which lets you focus entirely on your grip without managing a complicated recipe. This is intentional.

- **Protein:** Parmesan-crusted pasta
- **Veg:** Wilted baby spinach, sautéed in the pasta pan with a little olive oil, garlic, and lemon
- **No starch needed** — pasta is the base

-----

### Tuesday — The Claw

**Skill:** Before touching the stove, take one onion, one carrot, one celery stalk. Practice the claw grip — fingers curled, knuckles as the guide rail for the blade. Keep the tip of the blade in contact with the board, pull back slightly until the blade slices in, then press down and forward using the full length of the blade. Dice all three into rough ½” pieces. Time yourself. Don’t worry about speed yet — worry about uniformity.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — The Best Chicken Noodle Soup](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-chicken-noodle-soup-recipe)

A classic mirepoix — onion, carrot, celery — is the entire flavor base of this soup. Your Tuesday drill *is* the recipe’s most important step. Everything else follows from it.

- **Protein:** Chicken, simmered and shredded into the broth
- **Veg:** Mirepoix (your drill), plus parsley
- **Starch:** Egg noodles, cooked directly in the soup

-----

### Wednesday — Rock Chop vs. Push Cut

**Skill:** Mince a full head of garlic and a large bunch of flat-leaf parsley before you start cooking. Use two strokes deliberately — the rock chop (blade tip stays on the board, heel rocks) and the push cut (blade lifts fully, pushes through cleanly). Notice the difference in speed and texture. The rock chop gives control; the push cut gives speed. Both have their place.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Shrimp Scampi](https://www.seriouseats.com/shrimp-scampi-recipe)

Shrimp scampi is built almost entirely on well-minced garlic and parsley. Your drill is the most important prep step in the dish. The knife work makes or breaks the flavor here.

- **Protein:** Shrimp, sautéed in garlic butter and white wine
- **Veg:** A simple arugula salad — [Serious Eats Arugula Salad with Shaved Parmesan and Lemon](https://www.seriouseats.com/arugula-salad-with-shaved-parmesan-and-lemon-vinaigrette-recipe) — dressed while the shrimp rests
- **Starch:** Crusty bread or linguine

-----

### Thursday — OFF

📖 Read: [Serious Eats — How to Sharpen and Hone a Knife](https://www.seriouseats.com/knife-skills-how-to-sharpen-a-knife)

Is your knife actually sharp? A dull knife is the #1 reason home cook technique looks worse than it is. If you don’t own a honing steel, order one tonight.

-----

### Friday — Mise en Place

**Skill:** The real chef’s secret. Tonight, before you turn on a single burner, prep *everything* first. Every ingredient chopped, measured, and arranged in small bowls or on the cutting board. Then cook. Notice how the experience changes — you’re not scrambling, you’re assembling.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Stir-Fried Beef and Broccoli](https://www.seriouseats.com/stir-fried-beef-and-broccoli-with-oyster-sauce-recipe)

Stir-fries punish you for skipping mise en place. The heat is too high and things move too fast. This recipe *forces* you to have everything ready before the wok gets hot. It is the perfect mise en place teacher.

- **Protein:** Thinly sliced flank steak, velveted in the wok
- **Veg:** Broccoli florets, blanched then wok-tossed
- **Starch:** Steamed jasmine rice, started before you begin the stir-fry

-----

### Saturday — PROJECT MEAL: French Onion Soup

**Skill:** Slicing 4–5 lbs of onions into uniform thin half-moons. This takes 20+ minutes and teaches you more than any drill — because caramelization depends entirely on uniform thickness. Thin, even slices cook at the same rate. Uneven ones create pockets of raw and burned, and the whole dish suffers.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — French Onion Soup (Soupe à l’Oignon)](https://www.seriouseats.com/french-onion-soup-recipe)

Onions caramelized low and slow in butter until deeply golden, deglazed with wine, finished with beef stock. Topped with toasted garlic-rubbed bread and broiled Gruyère. A self-contained stunning dinner — nothing else needed except a glass of red wine.

*Skill targets: Uniform half-moon slices. Pinch grip maintained throughout. Knife honed before you start.*

-----

### Sunday — OFF

Write three sentences somewhere: What felt awkward this week? What clicked? What do you want to fix? Your notes app is fine. This three-sentence habit is worth more than any cooking class.

-----

## Week 2 — Cuts Have Names. Learn Them.

The grip is feeling more natural. Now we add vocabulary and intentionality to the cuts themselves.

-----

### Monday — The Julienne

**Skill:** Take two carrots and one zucchini. Square off each vegetable first (cut a flat face on one side so it doesn’t roll). Slice into planks. Stack the planks and cut into matchsticks — roughly 2–3 inches long, ⅛ inch thick. This is also the foundation of the brunoise (tiny dice). Go slow. Precision before speed.

*Why it matters:* Julienned vegetables cook faster and more evenly than rough cuts. They also look professional without any extra effort.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — The Best Pad Thai](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-pad-thai-recipe)

Julienned vegetables are threaded throughout. Your drill *is* the recipe prep — the uniform cut directly affects the final texture.

- **Protein:** Shrimp or chicken, or tofu
- **Veg:** Your julienned carrots and scallions, plus bean sprouts
- **Starch:** Rice noodles, soaked per the recipe

-----

### Tuesday — The Dice (Small, Medium, Large)

**Skill:** Dice three potatoes — one small (¼”), one medium (½”), one large (¾”). Notice the process: planks first, then batons, then cubes. It’s not a rough chop. Make them as uniform as you can. Then cook all three in the same hot pan at the same time with oil and salt. Watch what happens — the small ones finish first. The large ones are still raw when the small ones are done. That’s the entire lesson.

*Why it matters:* Uniform cuts = uniform cooking. This is the single biggest quality gap between home cooks and professionals.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Home Fries](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-home-fries-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Pan-Seared Crispy Chicken Thighs](https://www.seriouseats.com/crispy-pan-seared-chicken-thighs-recipe)

Use medium dice on the home fries for the recipe as written. The chicken thighs are a weeknight workhorse — learn this technique and you’ll use it for the rest of the program.

- **Protein:** Crispy pan-seared bone-in chicken thighs
- **Veg:** A simple green salad — [Serious Eats — Classic Green Salad with Vinaigrette](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette)
- **Starch:** Home fries (your dice drill is the recipe)

-----

### Wednesday — Breaking Down a Whole Chicken

**Skill:** Buy a whole chicken. Read [Serious Eats — How to Break Down a Chicken](https://www.seriouseats.com/knife-skills-how-to-break-down-a-chicken) first, then do it. You’ll get two bone-in breasts, two thighs, two drumsticks, two wings, and a carcass. Put the carcass in a zip-lock in the freezer — that’s Week 7 stock material. Follow the joints, not the bones. Let the knife find the natural seams.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Chicken Piccata](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-chicken-piccata-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Arugula Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/arugula-salad-with-shaved-parmesan-and-lemon-vinaigrette-recipe)

Use the breasts from your breakdown. The thighs and legs go in the fridge (use them Friday) or freeze for next week. Nothing wasted.

- **Protein:** Chicken piccata using the breasts you broke down
- **Veg / Salad:** Arugula with shaved parmesan and lemon vinaigrette
- **Starch:** Buttered egg noodles or mashed potatoes

-----

### Thursday — OFF

📖 Read: [Serious Eats — What Actually Dulls Your Knife](https://www.seriouseats.com/knife-skills-how-to-sharpen-a-knife)

The dishwasher, glass cutting boards, and storing knives loose in a drawer — these are the three biggest offenders. Check your own habits.

-----

### Friday — Speed Benchmark

**Skill:** Time yourself dicing one onion. Write it down. This is your baseline. At the end of Week 4, you’ll time yourself again. Speed is not the goal — having a reference point is.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Shakshuka](https://www.seriouseats.com/shakshuka-for-one-recipe) + leftover chicken thighs from Wednesday (warmed in a skillet)

Shakshuka requires dicing onion, pepper, and garlic as its entire prep — your speed drill is built into the recipe. The leftover thighs alongside make it a proper complete dinner.

- **Protein:** Eggs poached in the shakshuka sauce, plus Wednesday’s leftover chicken thighs
- **Veg:** Onion, bell pepper, tomatoes (the shakshuka base)
- **Starch:** Crusty bread for dipping

-----

### Saturday — PROJECT MEAL: Provençal Vegetable Tian

**Skill:** This is the most visually demanding knife project of the two weeks. Slice eggplant, zucchini, yellow squash, and tomatoes into rounds of *identical* thickness — ideally ⅛ to ¼ inch. The beauty of the finished dish is a direct function of your slicing precision. Uniform slices = a dish that looks like a restaurant made it. Uneven slices = obvious.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Provençal Summer Vegetable Tian](https://www.seriouseats.com/summer-vegetable-tian-ratatouille-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Green Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette) + crusty bread

The tian is vegetarian and stunning on its own. Add a proper green salad with a classic vinaigrette (you’ll get more practice on vinaigrette in Week 7) and good bread for a complete, beautiful dinner.

- **Protein:** Optional — serve with good burrata or a soft-boiled egg on top
- **Veg:** The tian (eggplant, zucchini, squash, tomato)
- **Salad:** Simple green salad with Dijon vinaigrette
- **Starch:** Crusty baguette

*Skill targets: Consistent thickness on every slice. Go slow. This is about control, not speed.*

-----

### Sunday — OFF

Compare this reflection to last week’s. The grip should feel natural. You should know the difference between a julienne, a small dice, and a rock chop — not just in theory, but in your hands. Somewhere this week you probably had a moment where the knife felt like an extension of your arm. Write it down.

# 🔥 WEEKS 3–4: Heat & Cooking Methods

> *“Understanding heat is understanding cooking. Everything else is detail.”*

-----

## Week 3 — Dry Heat: The Maillard Reaction Is Your Best Friend

**Before you cook anything this week**, read:

- 📖 [Serious Eats — An Introduction to the Maillard Reaction](https://www.seriouseats.com/what-is-maillard-reaction-cooking-science) — the science of browning, flavor, and why a dry, hot pan matters more than any spice blend

The core lesson: **dry heat above 300°F triggers the Maillard reaction** — the chemical cascade that creates crust, color, and hundreds of flavor compounds. Wet heat (steam, water) cannot get food above 212°F, which means it cannot trigger this reaction. This is why a boiled steak tastes nothing like a seared one.

-----

### Monday — Sauté: Hot Pan, Don’t Crowd

**Skill:** Sautéing means high heat, small amount of fat, food in a single layer, moving frequently. The most common home cook mistake: cold pan, crowded pan, food that steams instead of browns. Tonight, heat your pan until a drop of water evaporates immediately before adding oil. And do not crowd the pan — cook in batches if needed.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Sautéed Chicken Breasts with Quick Pan Sauce](https://www.seriouseats.com/sauteed-chicken-breast-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Sautéed Zucchini with Garlic and Herbs](https://www.seriouseats.com/sauteed-zucchini-recipe) + Steamed rice

- **Protein:** Sautéed boneless chicken breast, finished with a quick pan sauce of shallot, white wine, and butter
- **Veg:** Sautéed zucchini — notice how a hot pan gives you color on the outside while keeping the inside tender
- **Starch:** Steamed jasmine rice

-----

### Tuesday — Roasting: Hot Oven, Dry Surface

**Skill:** Roasting is dry oven heat that circulates around food, browning all exposed surfaces. The two secrets: (1) high heat (400°F+) and (2) a *dry* surface on the food. Pat everything dry. Never roast wet food — the moisture steams before it can brown.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Sheet Pan Chicken Thighs with Roasted Vegetables](https://www.seriouseats.com/sheet-pan-chicken-thighs-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Green Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette)

Use bone-in, skin-on thighs. Pat them completely dry before they go in the oven. Compare the color and crispness tonight to Wednesday’s braise — you’ll eat the difference.

- **Protein:** Bone-in skin-on chicken thighs, roasted until crackling
- **Veg:** Whatever is on the sheet pan — broccoli, potatoes, onions
- **Salad:** Simple green salad alongside

-----

### Wednesday — Braising: Low, Slow, Wet

**Skill:** Braising is a two-step method. First, sear the meat in a very hot pan (dry heat, Maillard reaction). Then, add a small amount of liquid, cover, and cook at low heat for a long time. The collagen in tough cuts breaks down into gelatin, making the meat spoon-tender and the braising liquid silky. Never rush a braise.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Braised Chicken Thighs with Tomatoes and Capers](https://www.seriouseats.com/braised-chicken-thighs-cacciatore) + Crusty bread or polenta

This is a weeknight-friendly braise — it cooks in about 45 minutes. Notice the difference in texture from Tuesday’s roasted thighs — same cut of meat, entirely different result because of heat and moisture.

- **Protein:** Braised chicken thighs in a rich tomato-caper sauce
- **Veg:** The braise liquid *is* the veg component — tomatoes, olives, herbs
- **Starch:** Crusty bread to mop the sauce, or polenta

-----

### Thursday — OFF

📖 Read: [Serious Eats — The Difference Between Sautéing, Frying, and Braising](https://www.seriouseats.com/guide-to-cooking-methods)

Identify which of the three methods you used this week produced the best flavor on the same cut of chicken, and why.

-----

### Friday — Steaming and Poaching: Gentle, Wet Heat

**Skill:** Wet heat (steaming and poaching) keeps food moist and tender but produces no browning. It’s ideal for delicate proteins and vegetables where you want to preserve texture and color. The trick: control your temperature. A hard boil beats up delicate proteins; a gentle simmer protects them.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Perfect Poached Salmon](https://www.seriouseats.com/perfect-poached-salmon-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Steamed Bok Choy with Garlic Sauce](https://www.seriouseats.com/steamed-bok-choy-with-garlic-sauce) + Steamed rice

- **Protein:** Gently poached salmon — silky, delicate, nothing like a seared fillet
- **Veg:** Steamed bok choy, dressed simply with garlic and sesame
- **Starch:** Steamed rice

-----

### Saturday — PROJECT MEAL: Spatchcocked Roast Chicken

**Skill:** Spatchcocking (butterflying) a whole chicken removes the backbone, flattens the bird, and allows it to roast evenly in about half the time of a trussed bird. You’ll use your knife skills from Weeks 1–2 (breaking down the backbone with kitchen shears or a chef’s knife) and your roasting knowledge from this week. The result: the crispiest skin you’ve ever made at home.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Spatchcocked Roast Chicken](https://www.seriouseats.com/spatchcocked-butterflied-roast-chicken-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Potatoes](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-roasted-potatoes-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Green Salad with Vinaigrette](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette)

A complete Sunday-worthy dinner cooked on a Saturday. The drippings from the chicken are the base for a pan sauce — don’t skip it.

- **Protein:** Whole spatchcocked chicken, roasted at high heat until golden and crackling
- **Veg / Starch:** Crispy smashed roasted potatoes cooked under the chicken (they absorb all the drippings)
- **Salad:** Simple green salad with a sharp Dijon vinaigrette

*Skill targets: Even butterflying, dry skin before roasting, proper resting time (10 minutes) before carving.*

## Week 4 — Dry Heat Mastery + The Benchmark Cook

> This week you’ll revisit dry-heat methods with beef and fish, and end with your first real benchmark: a complete multi-component dinner cooked with no recipes open in front of you.

-----

### Monday — Searing Steak: The Art of Doing Nothing

**Skill:** Put the steak in a screaming hot cast iron pan. Now don’t touch it. The instinct to move, prod, and flip food constantly is the enemy of a good sear. Let the Maillard reaction happen. The steak will release from the pan when it’s ready to flip. If you have to force it, it’s not ready.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Perfect Pan-Seared Steak with Butter Basting](https://www.seriouseats.com/perfect-pan-seared-steaks-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Creamy Mashed Potatoes](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-mashed-potatoes-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Sautéed Green Beans](https://www.seriouseats.com/sauteed-green-beans-recipe)

- **Protein:** Ribeye or NY strip, pan-seared in cast iron, basted in butter, thyme, and garlic
- **Starch:** Ultra-creamy mashed potatoes (use more butter than you’re comfortable with)
- **Veg:** Green beans sautéed in the steak pan drippings

-----

### Tuesday — High-Heat Fish: Crispy Skin Without Fear

**Skill:** Fish with crispy skin requires a hot, *well-oiled* pan and — again — patience. Press the fish gently against the pan for the first 30 seconds to prevent curling. Then leave it alone. Cook 80% of the way on the skin side. Flip only once, finish for 60 seconds on the flesh side.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Crispy Pan-Seared Salmon](https://www.seriouseats.com/crispy-pan-seared-salmon-fillet-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Asparagus](https://www.seriouseats.com/roasted-asparagus-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Arugula and Fennel Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/arugula-salad-with-shaved-parmesan-and-lemon-vinaigrette-recipe)

- **Protein:** Crispy-skinned salmon fillet, finished with lemon and capers
- **Veg:** Roasted asparagus (high-heat oven, single layer, very dry)
- **Salad:** Arugula salad alongside

-----

### Wednesday — Broiling: Overhead Heat

**Skill:** Broiling is essentially an upside-down grill — radiant heat comes from above. It’s ideal for finishing dishes (melting cheese, charring a surface) or for cooking thin items quickly. The key: keep the rack close to the element and watch constantly. Broilers operate at max heat and burn food in seconds.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Broiled Salmon with Miso Glaze](https://www.seriouseats.com/easy-broiled-miso-glazed-fish-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Sautéed Bok Choy](https://www.seriouseats.com/steamed-bok-choy-with-garlic-sauce) + Steamed rice

- **Protein:** Miso-glazed salmon under the broiler — 8 minutes, no flipping needed
- **Veg:** Sautéed or steamed bok choy
- **Starch:** Steamed rice

-----

### Thursday — OFF

⏱️ **Speed benchmark:** Dice one onion and time yourself. Compare to Week 2 Friday. Write both numbers down. How much faster are you? Has your uniformity improved or suffered?

📖 Read: [Serious Eats — A Complete Guide to Cooking Methods](https://www.seriouseats.com/guide-to-cooking-methods)

### Friday — Recap Cook: No Recipes

**Skill challenge:** Tonight you cook a full meal using only methods you’ve learned in Weeks 3–4, but with no recipe open. Pick a protein in your fridge. Choose a heat method. Season it. Make a simple sauce from the pan drippings. Cook a vegetable. Plate it.

If you need to glance at times and temperatures, that’s fine. But close the recipe tab.

This is the week’s most important session.

-----

### Saturday — PROJECT MEAL: Braised Short Ribs

**Skill:** The full braise from start to finish. This is a long cook (3–4 hours) but almost entirely passive — you sear the ribs in batches, build the braising liquid, and let the oven do the rest. The hardest part is the patience. The reward is one of the most spectacular things you can put on a plate.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Braised Short Ribs](https://www.seriouseats.com/braised-short-ribs-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Creamy Polenta](https://www.seriouseats.com/creamy-polenta-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Root Vegetables](https://www.seriouseats.com/roasted-root-vegetables-recipe)

- **Protein:** Bone-in beef short ribs braised in red wine and beef stock until they collapse
- **Starch:** Creamy parmesan polenta (the perfect landing pad for the braising liquid)
- **Veg:** Roasted root vegetables (carrots, parsnips, turnips)

*Skill targets: A deep, dark sear on every side of the ribs before braising. Correct liquid level (one-third up the meat, not submerged). Internal temp of 200°F+ for collagen breakdown.*

# 🥣 WEEKS 5–6: The 5 Mother Sauces

> *“Master these five sauces and you can make virtually any sauce on earth.”*

-----

**Before you start Week 5**, read:

- 📖 [Serious Eats — The 5 French Mother Sauces, Explained](https://www.seriouseats.com/all-about-the-mother-sauces)

The five mother sauces are: **Béchamel** (milk + roux), **Velouté** (stock + roux), **Espagnole** (dark stock + roux + tomato), **Hollandaise** (emulsified egg yolk + butter), and **Sauce Tomat** (tomato-based). Four out of five start with a roux. Learn the roux and you learn four sauces at once.

-----

## Week 5 — Roux-Based Sauces

-----

### Monday — Béchamel: The White Sauce

**Skill:** A béchamel is a white roux (equal parts butter and flour, cooked briefly until it smells nutty but hasn’t browned) whisked with warm milk. The key is adding the milk gradually while whisking constantly, and cooking it long enough to lose the raw flour taste. Season with salt, white pepper, and a pinch of nutmeg.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Baked Mac and Cheese (Béchamel-Based)](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-food-lab-the-best-baked-macaroni-and-cheese) + [Serious Eats — Simple Green Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette)

You’ll make the béchamel from scratch and transform it into a Mornay sauce (béchamel + cheese). Then it becomes mac and cheese. This is the mother sauce → daughter sauce chain in action.

- **Protein:** Mac and cheese is the whole dish
- **Starch:** Pasta (built-in)
- **Salad:** Simple green salad to cut through the richness

-----

### Tuesday — Velouté: Stock Instead of Milk

**Skill:** A velouté starts with the same white roux as béchamel but uses chicken (or fish, or veal) stock instead of milk. The result is lighter, more savory, and more versatile as a sauce base. It should coat a spoon lightly when done — if it’s too thick, thin it with more warm stock.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Chicken Pot Pie with Velouté Base](https://www.seriouseats.com/chicken-pot-pie-recipe) + Simple green salad

The pot pie filling is a classic chicken velouté extended with vegetables and cream. You’re learning the mother sauce *and* a classic comfort food simultaneously.

- **Protein:** Chicken (cooked and pulled into the filling)
- **Starch:** The pastry crust
- **Veg:** Peas, carrots, and onion in the filling
- **Salad:** Simple green salad alongside

-----

### Wednesday — Sauce Tomat: The Slow Tomato

**Skill:** The mother tomato sauce is not marinara. It’s a deeply flavored, long-cooked sauce built on a pork fat base, mirepoix, tomatoes, stock, and aromatics. The key is patience — low heat, long time, and tasting as the sauce develops. Learn to season in layers, not all at once.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — The Best Slow-Cooked Bolognese](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-slow-cooked-bolognese-sauce-with-fresh-pasta-recipe)

Bolognese is a tomato-based meat sauce that takes 3–4 hours. Make it on a Wednesday when you can let it simmer while doing other things. It rewards patience with a depth of flavor no quick sauce can match.

- **Protein:** Ground beef and pork, slow-cooked into the sauce
- **Starch:** Fresh tagliatelle or good dried pasta
- **Veg:** Mirepoix (already in the sauce) + a simple salad alongside: [Serious Eats — Classic Caesar Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/best-classic-caesar-salad-recipe)

-----

### Thursday — OFF

📖 Read: [Food52 — The Five Mother Sauces Every Cook Should Know](https://food52.com/story/12209-the-five-mother-sauces-every-cook-should-know)

-----

### Friday — Espagnole and Demi-Glace (Simplified)

**Skill:** Espagnole (Spanish sauce) is a *brown* roux-based sauce made with dark beef stock and tomato. The demi-glace is espagnole reduced by half until it becomes intensely flavored and glossy. This is the sauce that French restaurant pan sauces descend from. Tonight you make a simplified version — no days-long reduction, but the same concept.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Steak with Red Wine Pan Sauce](https://www.seriouseats.com/perfect-pan-seared-steaks-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Creamy Mashed Potatoes](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-mashed-potatoes-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Broccolini](https://www.seriouseats.com/roasted-broccolini-recipe)

The pan sauce is your simplified Espagnole: the browned bits from the steak (Maillard), deglazed with wine, reduced with stock. It tastes like a restaurant made it because it *is* the technique restaurants use.

- **Protein:** Pan-seared steak with a red wine reduction pan sauce
- **Starch:** Ultra-creamy mashed potatoes
- **Veg:** Roasted broccolini

-----

### Saturday — PROJECT MEAL: Moussaka (Béchamel + Sauce Tomat)

## Week 6 — Emulsified Sauces + Vinaigrette

-----

### Monday — Hollandaise: The Emulsion

**Skill:** Hollandaise is the most technically demanding of the five mother sauces — it’s an emulsion of egg yolks and clarified butter, stabilized by the lecithin in the yolks. The keys: gentle heat (too hot and the eggs scramble), constant whisking, and adding the butter slowly. Use a double boiler and your thermometer tonight.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Eggs Benedict with Hollandaise](https://www.seriouseats.com/eggs-benedict-recipe) — for dinner. Yes, breakfast for dinner is legitimate, especially when it teaches you hollandaise.

- **Protein:** Canadian bacon, poached eggs
- **Sauce:** Hollandaise (your skill for tonight)
- **Starch:** English muffins, toasted
- **Veg / Salad:** [Serious Eats — Simple Watercress Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/watercress-salad-recipe) alongside

-----

### Tuesday — Mayonnaise: Emulsion Without Heat

**Skill:** Mayo is a cold emulsion — egg yolk, acid (lemon or vinegar), and oil, whisked until stable. It teaches you the same principle as hollandaise (fat suspended in water-based liquid via lecithin) but is much more forgiving. Start with a small amount of oil and add it *very slowly* at first to establish the emulsion before adding more quickly.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Lobster-Style Rolls with Homemade Mayo](https://www.seriouseats.com/lobster-rolls-recipe) (use whatever seafood you like — shrimp and scallop rolls work perfectly) + [Serious Eats — Classic Coleslaw](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-creamy-coleslaw-recipe)

Make the mayo from scratch. Use it in the roll filling and the coleslaw.

- **Protein:** Shrimp or scallop roll (or lobster if you’re feeling it)
- **Veg:** Coleslaw made with your homemade mayo
- **Starch:** Brioche or potato rolls

-----

### Wednesday — The Vinaigrette: A Temporary Emulsion

**Skill:** A vinaigrette is an unstable emulsion — oil and acid mixed together, which separate unless stabilized with an emulsifier like Dijon mustard or honey. The classic ratio is 3:1 oil to acid. Taste it on a piece of bread (not a spoon) to understand how it will actually taste on food.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Pan-Seared Salmon Niçoise Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/salade-nicoise-recipe) — this is a full composed dinner salad, not a side.

Make the vinaigrette from scratch. Use it as the dressing and as a marinade for the tuna or salmon.

- **Protein:** Seared salmon or tuna
- **Veg:** Green beans, olives, tomatoes, hard-boiled eggs
- **Starch:** Boiled potatoes (dressed warm with the vinaigrette — they absorb it better)

📖 Read: [Serious Eats — How to Make Any Salad Dressing Without a Recipe](https://www.seriouseats.com/vinaigrette-salad-dressing-recipe)

### Friday — Pan Sauce: The Working Sauce

**Skill:** A pan sauce isn’t one of the five mother sauces, but it’s descended from all of them. After searing a protein, you deglaze the pan (scraping the fond — those dark caramelized bits), reduce a liquid (wine, stock), and finish with butter. This is how restaurants make almost every sauce on their menu.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Pork Chops with Apple-Cider Pan Sauce](https://www.seriouseats.com/pan-seared-pork-chops-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Sweet Potatoes](https://www.seriouseats.com/roasted-sweet-potato-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Green Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette)

- **Protein:** Pan-seared pork chops with apple-cider pan sauce
- **Starch:** Roasted sweet potatoes
- **Salad:** Simple green salad with your from-scratch vinaigrette (Week 6 callback)

## Week 7 — Stocks & Broths

**Before you start**, read:

- 📖 [Serious Eats — The Difference Between Stock and Broth](https://www.seriouseats.com/ask-the-food-lab-whats-the-difference-between-stock-and-broth)

**The Week 7 rule:** Get your frozen chicken carcass from Week 2 out of the freezer Sunday night. You’re going to use it.

-----

### Monday — Chicken Stock from Scratch

**Skill:** Cold water start, gentle simmer (never boil), skim the foam in the first 20 minutes, don’t stir. A good stock simmers for 3–4 hours. It should be golden, clear (not murky), and taste of chicken without being aggressively salty. Cool it in an ice bath, then refrigerate. A proper stock should gel when cold — that’s the gelatin from the bones.

This takes time but is mostly passive. Start it when you get home, let it simmer while you do other things.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Quick Weeknight Ramen Using Homemade Stock](https://www.seriouseats.com/easy-ramen-recipe) — use your stock as the base

- **Protein:** Soft-boiled eggs (a preview of next week), chashu pork or a soft-boiled egg, scallions
- **Starch:** Ramen noodles
- **Broth:** Your homemade chicken stock, seasoned with soy and mirin

-----

### Tuesday — Pan Sauces from Stock

**Skill:** Now that you have fresh stock, you understand why pan sauces taste so much better when made with real stock instead of water or canned broth. The gelatin creates body and silkiness that nothing else can replicate. Tonight’s lesson: the difference is in your freezer.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Chicken with Rich Pan Sauce](https://www.seriouseats.com/sauteed-chicken-breast-recipe) using your homemade stock + [Serious Eats — Roasted Potatoes](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-roasted-potatoes-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Bitter Greens Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/bitter-greens-salad-recipe)

- **Protein:** Sautéed chicken with a pan sauce made from your Week 7 stock
- **Starch:** Crispy roasted potatoes
- **Salad:** Bitter greens salad with a sharp vinaigrette

-----

### Wednesday — Fish Stock + Fumet

**Skill:** Fish stock (fumet) takes only 20–30 minutes — fish bones give up their gelatin quickly and become bitter if cooked too long. Ask your fishmonger for frames (carcasses) or use shrimp shells. The aromatics are lighter: white wine, leek, fennel, parsley stems, bay leaf.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Bouillabaisse](https://www.seriouseats.com/best-bouillabaisse-recipe) — the classic Provençal fish stew, built on your fresh fish stock

- **Protein:** Mixed seafood — shrimp, clams, white fish
- **Stock:** Your fresh fish fumet
- **Starch:** Grilled bread rubbed with garlic (rouille on the side)
- **Veg:** Fennel, leek, tomato in the stew

-----

### Thursday — OFF

📖 Read: [Serious Eats — How to Make Restaurant-Quality Stock at Home](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-rich-flavorful-chicken-stock)

-----

### Friday — Vegetable Stock + Risotto

**Skill:** Vegetable stock is the quickest and most approachable. The key is not to over-simmer it — 45–60 minutes max, or it turns bitter. Tonight you’ll use it immediately for risotto, which also teaches you another core technique: the gradual addition of hot liquid while stirring to coax starch from Arborio rice.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Parmesan Risotto](https://www.seriouseats.com/perfect-risotto-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Mushrooms on Top](https://www.seriouseats.com/roasted-mushroom-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Simple Arugula Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/arugula-salad-with-shaved-parmesan-and-lemon-vinaigrette-recipe)

- **Protein:** Roasted mushrooms (the star) + parmesan as umami
- **Starch:** Risotto (this is the main dish)
- **Salad:** Arugula alongside to cut through the richness

## Week 8 — Eggs

> *“A chef is judged by their eggs.”*

**Before you start**, read:

- 📖 [Serious Eats — The Egg Files: Everything You Need to Know](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-egg-files-the-definitive-guide-to-eggs)

### Monday — The French Scramble

**Skill:** French scrambled eggs are not American scrambled eggs. They’re cooked low and slow, constantly stirred with a spatula, off and on the heat, until they form soft, creamy, barely-set curds. The result looks like nothing you’ve ever made in the morning, and tastes like what restaurants serve for brunch at $22 a plate.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — French Scrambled Eggs](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-perfect-scrambled-eggs) + [Serious Eats — Smoked Salmon with Crème Fraîche](https://www.seriouseats.com/smoked-salmon-spread-recipe) + Toasted sourdough + [Serious Eats — Simple Greens Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette)

A French scramble dinner is a legitimately elegant meal when served with smoked salmon and a sharp green salad.

- **Protein:** French scrambled eggs + smoked salmon
- **Starch:** Toasted sourdough
- **Salad:** Simple green salad

-----

### Tuesday — The Perfect Poached Egg

**Skill:** Fresh eggs, a wide shallow pan, simmering (not boiling) water with a splash of vinegar, a gentle swirl, and confidence. The white should be fully set and the yolk should run when broken. Most home cooks are afraid of poached eggs — by the end of tonight, you shouldn’t be.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Poached Eggs Over Sautéed Greens with Crispy Bacon](https://www.seriouseats.com/poached-egg-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Roasted Tomatoes](https://www.seriouseats.com/slow-roasted-cherry-tomatoes-recipe) + Toasted bread

- **Protein:** Poached eggs + crispy bacon
- **Veg:** Wilted greens (kale or spinach), roasted cherry tomatoes
- **Starch:** Good toast

-----

### Wednesday — The Omelette

**Skill:** The French omelette is one of the hardest things in cooking to get right — pale, barely colored outside, softly set inside, folded into a cylinder in under 2 minutes. The American version (fully cooked, browned, folded in half) is easier. Practice the French version until you get it right. Make two omelettes — one for you, one for improvement.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — The French Omelette](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-french-omelette) + [Serious Eats — Classic Green Salad](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-make-salad-vinaigrette) + Crusty bread

A properly made omelette with a glass of wine and a green salad is one of the most satisfying dinners in existence.

- **Protein:** Two omelettes (one per person or two for you)
- **Filling:** Gruyère, herbs, or mushrooms
- **Salad:** Classic green salad with vinaigrette
- **Starch:** Crusty bread

### Friday — Custard: Eggs as Thickener

**Skill:** When eggs are heated in liquid (cream, milk), the proteins set and thicken the mixture into a custard. The key is temperature control — too high and the eggs scramble, too low and it never sets. A proper custard coats the back of a spoon and holds a line when you drag your finger through it.

**Full Meal:** [Serious Eats — Pasta Carbonara](https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-pasta-carbonara-recipe) + [Serious Eats — Bitter Greens Salad with Anchovy Vinaigrette](https://www.seriouseats.com/bitter-greens-salad-recipe)

Carbonara is a savory egg custard sauce — eggs, pasta water, guanciale or pancetta, and pecorino. The pasta water (starchy, hot) tempers the eggs and keeps them from scrambling. This dish teaches custard technique without making an actual dessert custard.

- **Protein:** Guanciale or pancetta (built into the sauce)
- **Starch:** Spaghetti or rigatoni
- **Salad:** Bitter greens with anchovy vinaigrette
