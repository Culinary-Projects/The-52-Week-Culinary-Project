    🐟 Block 21–22: Whole Fish, Curing & Advanced Seafood

    ← Block 19–20: Flatfish & Round Fish | Block 23–24: Fish — Advanced →

    "Curing a fish is the most intimate act of preservation — you are changing its character with nothing but salt, time, and intention. The result tastes like the sea remembered."

    Before You Start Block 21

    What this block adds: Block 19–20 built your core fish-cooking technique — pan-searing, meunière, miso glaze, whole fish in the oven. Block 21–22 goes further: salt curing, trout Amandine (a different whole-fish technique), grilled whole fish, mussels, crab cakes, and cioppino. By the end you will have a complete seafood vocabulary.

    ⏰ Planning Ahead

    • Session 1 (Gravlax): start the cure 48–72 hrs before Session 3 — it comes out of the fridge tasting transformed
    • Session 8 (Cioppino): ask your fishmonger 2 days ahead to save fish bones/shells for the stock

    Session 0 — Breaking Down a Whole Fish (optional — do this if you didn't in Block 19)

    Skill: Filleting a whole round fish — the most useful fish skill in the kitchen.

    📖 Read: Serious Eats — How to Fillet a Whole Fish

    Buy one whole branzino or snapper. Fillet both sides, remove pin bones, and cook the fillets simply (pan-sear with butter and lemon). Eat the result knowing you got more fish for less money and it was fresher.

    The economics: A whole 2 lb branzino costs $12–14 at a good fishmonger. The same fish sold as fillets: $18–22. You just learned where $6–8 of the price comes from.

    Block 21 — Whole Fish & Curing

    The central principle: Whole fish and cured fish are the two preparations that demand attention to the fish itself — its freshness, its age, its smell — in a way that a fillet from a vacuum pack never will. This week teaches you to read fish.

    Session 1 — Gravlax: Salt, Sugar, and Time

    Skill: Dry curing fish (no heat, no smoke) — the Scandinavian technique for transforming fresh salmon with nothing but salt, sugar, and dill.

    The cure penetrates the flesh over 48–72 hours, drawing out moisture, firming the texture, and intensifying flavor. The result tastes like the best smoked salmon you've ever had — except it's not smoked at all. Salt did everything.

    📖 Read: BBC Good Food — Gravadlax — the classic recipe and ratio (2:1 salt:sugar by weight, heaped with dill, weighted in the fridge)

    The technique: - Score the skin side of a 500g–1kg salmon fillet lightly - Mix coarse salt and sugar (2:1 ratio salt:sugar by weight) - Press the cure onto both sides, cover with plenty of fresh dill - Place in a dish skin-side down, weigh with another dish and some cans, refrigerate - Flip every 12 hours; cure 48–72 hours - Remove, scrape off the dill and cure (don't rinse), and slice thinly on the bias

    Note: Start the cure tonight (Session 1). It will be ready to slice by Session 3 or 4.

    Full Meal: - Begin gravlax cure — no dinner from this tonight - Quick pan-seared salmon for Session 1 dinner (compare texture now against cured version in two days)

    Session 2 — Trout Amandine: Whole Fish + Brown Butter

    Skill: Cooking a whole fish in butter — dredged in flour, pan-fried until golden, finished with a classic brown butter and toasted almond sauce. One of the most elegant French preparations, and fast.

    📖 Read: Serious Eats — Trout Amandine

    Trout Amandine teaches you to read a whole fish for doneness (the flesh should pull away from the bone at the dorsal fin; the eye turns white and opaque). The sauce — beurre noisette with toasted almonds and lemon juice — is made in the same pan in 90 seconds after the fish comes out.

    Full Meal: Trout Amandine + steamed haricots verts + crusty bread to catch the sauce

    Component Notes
    Fish Whole rainbow trout, butterflied or pan-ready
    Sauce Beurre noisette + toasted sliced almonds + lemon juice + parsley
    Veg Haricots verts, blanched or simply steamed

    🎥 Compare Notes: Sole Meunière (Jacques Pépin) — Different fish, identical technique — watch the brown butter timing and how little time separates noisette from burned.

    Session 3 — Taste the Gravlax / Cured Fish Board

    Skill: Slicing cured fish, assembling a Scandinavian board, tasting your own cure.

    Today you slice your gravlax (if it went in at Session 1) and taste it alongside store-bought smoked salmon — the comparison teaches you what curing does.

    Slicing: thin, long bias cuts with a very sharp flexible knife, starting from the tail end. Lay slices flat.

    Full Meal: - Cured salmon board: your gravlax + cream cheese or crème fraîche + capers + diced red onion + rye bread or blinis - Mustard-dill sauce alongside (Dijon + honey + white wine vinegar + neutral oil + fresh dill)

    Session 4 — Whole Grilled Branzino

    Skill: Small whole fish on the grill — scored, rubbed with olive oil, placed over direct high heat, flipped once. The bones protect the flesh from drying, the skin chars beautifully, and the result is cleaner-tasting than almost any fillet preparation.

    📖 Read: Serious Eats — Whole Grilled Fish

    Score the fish 3–4 times on each side (diagonal cuts to the bone — these allow heat to reach the thickest parts). Rub with oil, stuff the cavity with lemon slices, garlic, and herbs. Grill 4–5 minutes per side for a 500g fish over direct high heat.

    Full Meal: Whole grilled branzino + Italian salsa verde + roasted potatoes + green salad

    Component Notes
    Fish Whole branzino or snapper, 500g each
    Sauce Salsa verde made ahead: parsley, capers, anchovy, olive oil, lemon
    Starch Smashed and oven-crisped potatoes

    🎥 Compare Notes: How to Grill and Eat a Whole Fish (Helen Rennie) — Pay attention to how she separates the fillet from the bone at the table — that finishing move makes whole fish feel confident.

    ⏰ Session 5 — PROJECT: Whole Fish Dinner Party

    The challenge: A complete dinner centered on whole fish — plated with intention. Cook for at least two people.

    Full Meal (3 courses):

    1. Starter: Gravlax board (leftover from Session 3, or freshly sliced) with blinis, crème fraîche, capers, and pickled onion
    2. Main: Salt-Baked Whole Fish — the enclosed salt crust steams the fish in its own moisture; crack at the table. Dress the revealed fish with olive oil and capers.
    3. Side: Roasted potatoes + gremolata or salsa verde alongside

    Skill targets: A properly sealed salt crust (no cracks before roasting). Fish pulled at 130–135°F at the thickest point. Confident tableside crack-and-reveal. Graceful plating directly from the fish to the plate.

    Block 22 — Advanced Seafood: Shellfish & Stews

    "Shellfish are the most immediate form of the sea — they still smell like the ocean when they're alive, and they cook in minutes. Knowing how to handle them is knowing how to be present."

    Session 6 — Mussels Marinière: The French Classic

    Skill: Cooking shellfish in wine steam — the fastest impressive dish in this program. Two pounds of mussels, cleaned and debearded, cooked in white wine and aromatics for 6 minutes. The key: don't drown them in too much liquid. A thin layer of wine and aromatics in the bottom of the pot creates enough steam to open every shell.

    📖 Read: BBC Good Food — Moules Marinière

    Cleaning mussels: Pull off any beards right before cooking. Discard mussels that don't close when tapped sharply. Discard any that don't open after cooking.

    Full Meal: Mussels marinière + crusty bread (the entire purpose of which is to soak up the cooking liquid — make extra) + simple green salad

    Component Notes
    Mussels 2 lbs per person; scrubbed, debearded right before cooking
    Broth 1 cup dry white wine + 2 shallots + 3 garlic cloves + parsley stems
    Bread Sourdough or baguette — non-optional

    Session 7 — Crab Cakes: Restraint Is the Technique

    Skill: The test of a good crab cake is the ratio of crab to filler. Most crab cakes are bread cakes. A proper crab cake is barely held together — the crab is the thing, the binder is just enough to keep it cohesive.

    📖 Read: Simply Recipes — Crab Cakes

    Full Meal: Crab cakes + aioli or remoulade + coleslaw or quick-pickled red onion + simple green salad

    Component Notes
    Crab cakes 85–90% crab; minimal binder (egg, mayo, small amount of panko)
    Sauce Aioli (Block 5–6 technique) or a simple remoulade
    Side Coleslaw or herb salad — cool, acidic, a crisp counterpoint

    Buying crabmeat: Fresh or pasteurized lump crabmeat is the practical choice. Pick through carefully for shell fragments before mixing.

    ⏰ Session 8 — Cioppino: The Italian-American Fish Stew

    Skill: Building a great fish stew from a tomato-shellfish broth — the technique that integrates shellfish and fish at different add times based on their individual cook times.

    📖 Read: Simply Recipes — Cioppino

    Cioppino was invented by Italian immigrant fishermen in San Francisco — a tomato-and-wine stew into which firm fish goes first (10–12 minutes), then shellfish in order of cook time (clams and mussels first, shrimp last). The broth is the soul: deeply seasoned with fish bones and shellfish shells if you can get them.

    Plan ahead: Ask your fishmonger 2 days ahead to save fish frames/bones — they're usually free. Simmer them in water with aromatics for 20 minutes = a quick fumet that transforms the broth.

    Full Meal: Cioppino with fish, clams, mussels, and shrimp + crusty sourdough bread + simple salad

    Component Notes
    Broth Tomato + white wine + fish stock/fumet + saffron (optional)
    Fish (first in) Firm white fish — cod, halibut (10–12 min)
    Shellfish (last) Clams, mussels (6–8 min), shrimp (2–3 min)
    Bread Thick slices of sourdough, toasted

    🎥 Compare Notes: Bouillabaisse and How to Make Fish Stock (Helen Rennie) — The technique for building shellfish stock is identical; watch how she extracts maximum flavor from bones and shells before anything else goes into the pot.

    Session 9 — Miso-Glazed Black Cod (or Salmon)

    Skill: The Nobu preparation — white miso, mirin, sake, and sugar applied to black cod or salmon and left to cure overnight. The sugars in the miso caramelize under the broiler in 4 minutes.

    📖 Read: Serious Eats — Miso-Glazed Salmon

    Black cod (sablefish) is the traditional fish for this preparation — its high fat content handles the sweet glaze without drying out. Salmon is the more accessible substitute. Start the cure tonight or apply it two nights ahead.

    Timing: Apply glaze → refrigerate 24–48 hrs → broil 4–5 minutes on one side only → done.

    Full Meal: Miso-glazed cod or salmon + steamed rice + wilted bok choy or spinach + quick-pickled cucumbers

    Session 10 — PROJECT: Seafood Feast

    The challenge: A generous seafood dinner that pulls together everything from this block. This is the dinner you serve to impress — or eat alone on a Friday when you feel like celebrating.

    Full Meal:

    1. Starter: Mussels marinière (Session 6 technique) — a small portion to start, with bread
    2. Main: Cioppino (Session 8 — a larger pot made the same evening, or made a day ahead and reheated, which makes it richer)
    3. Side: Roasted potatoes or polenta alongside the cioppino
    4. Dessert: Whatever you feel like — this meal doesn't need more complexity

    Skill targets: Clean mussel broth (not watery or muddy). Cioppino with distinct, properly-cooked fish and shellfish — nothing rubbery, nothing falling apart. The whole table smells like the sea.

    Optional: Go Deeper

    Whole Fish: Why the Bones Matter

    A bone-in whole fish cooks more slowly and more evenly than a fillet — the bones conduct heat and protect the flesh from the outside temperature. Once you understand this, you reach for whole fish differently.

    • 📖 How to Fillet a Whole Fish — The knife skill that makes buying whole fish practical.

    Curing: Beyond Gravlax

    Gravlax is the entry point. The same principle (salt draws moisture, concentrates flavor, transforms texture) applies throughout Block 27–28 (Charcuterie). Once you understand salt curing, you start to see it everywhere.

    • 📖 BBC Good Food — Gravadlax — The full recipe, including the mustard-dill sauce that's traditional with it.

    Cioppino vs. Bouillabaisse

    Cioppino and bouillabaisse use almost identical technique but diverge in flavor profile: cioppino is tomato-forward (Italian-American), while bouillabaisse relies on saffron, fennel, and a Provençal rouille. Both teach building a shellfish broth. Once you've made one, the other follows logically.

    • 📖 Serious Eats — Bouillabaisse Marseillaise — If cioppino has you curious about the French version.

    A Book Worth Having

    • 📚 The Whole Fish Cookbook by Josh Niland — Argues for cooking the entire fish — the cheeks, the collar, the roe. Transforms how you see value and waste at the fish counter.
