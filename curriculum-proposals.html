    Curriculum Proposals: Gap Analysis & Repetition Review

    Status: Proposals only — no curriculum files have been changed.
    Sources used: The Professional Chef (CIA, 9th ed.) full text, verified against actual recipe lists extracted from the archive.org copy. All proposed free-source links are to Serious Eats, The Woks of Life, Red House Spice, or similar freely accessible sites — no CIA recipes used.

    Part 1 — CIA Chapter-by-Chapter Assessment

    The CIA Professional Chef is organized into 35 chapters grouped into six parts. Below, every chapter is compared against the current 26-block curriculum. Ratings: Strong / Adequate / Thin / Absent.

    Part I – The Profession (Ch 1–5)

    CIA Chapter Topic Curriculum Coverage Rating
    Ch 1 The Culinary Professional Block 01-02 intro text only Absent
    Ch 2 Menus & Recipes Block 15-16 midpoint review (partial) Thin
    Ch 3 Nutrition Not covered anywhere Absent
    Ch 4 Food Safety Block 01-02 has one external FDA link Absent
    Ch 5 Equipment Named throughout but no dedicated session Absent

    Notes:
    - Nutrition and food safety are not mere professional formalities — they affect every dish decision (salt, fat, temperature). Worth at least one session each.
    - Equipment identification (mandoline, spider, tamis, thermometers, scales) is currently handled by brief bullet mentions. A single session framing why each tool matters would pay dividends in later blocks.

    Part II – Mise en Place (Ch 6–16)

    CIA Chapter Topic Curriculum Coverage Rating
    Ch 6 Stocks Block 07-08, full session Strong
    Ch 7 Poultry Block 03-04 (roasting), world cuisines Adequate
    Ch 8 Beef & Veal Blocks 03-04, 15-16, 17-18 Strong
    Ch 9 Lamb Incidental (Block 39-40 Indian, 41-42 Middle Eastern) Thin
    Ch 10 Pork Block 25-26 Adequate
    Ch 11 Fish & Shellfish Blocks 19-20, 21-22 Adequate
    Ch 12 (Stocks, cont.) Already counted in Ch 6 —
    Ch 13 Sauces Block 05-06 (mother sauces) Adequate
    Ch 14 Soups Scattered individual dishes — no framework Thin
    Ch 15 MeP — Spice Blends & Marinades Incidental in world cuisine blocks Thin
    Ch 16 (additional stocks/MeP) — —

    Detailed notes:

    Ch 13 — Sauces. CIA covers: jus lié, demi-glace, espagnole, velouté (Supreme, Fish, Shrimp, Vegetable), béchamel (Mornay, Cheddar), tomato + Bolognese, béarnaise + choron + paloise, hollandaise + mousseline + maltaise, beurre blanc, compound butters (8 varieties), pesto, red pepper coulis.
    Block 05-06 covers the five mother sauces but beurre blanc, compound butters, and pan-sauce derivatives (marchand de vin, sherry vinegar, sauce Robert/charcutière) are absent. These are exactly the sauces used to finish grilled and sautéed proteins later in the curriculum.

    Ch 14 — Soups. CIA chapter structure: consommé (beef, royale), broths (all proteins), cream soups (tomato, broccoli, asparagus, celery), chowders (New England clam, Manhattan clam, corn, Pacific seafood), purée soups (lentil, split pea, black bean, Senate bean), bisques (shrimp, lobster), gumbo, cold soups (gazpacho, vichyssoise), global soups (miso, Thai coconut, tom yum, wonton, minestrone, Tuscan white bean, tortilla).
    The curriculum has individual soup dishes scattered across blocks but teaches no soup taxonomy. A learner completing the curriculum could make onion soup and bouillabaisse but would not understand the difference between a bisque, a chowder, a cream soup, and a purée soup or why consommé requires clarification.

    Ch 15 — MeP for Meats. CIA teaches spice blends as a library: garam masala, Chinese five-spice, BBQ blend, chili blend, curry blend, quatre épices, fines herbes — plus curry pastes (red, green, yellow) and marinades (Asian, BBQ, fish, game, mojo, teriyaki, citrus). The curriculum treats these incidentally. No session teaches spice blend construction or the principle of a dry rub vs. a wet marinade vs. a paste.

    Part III – Cooking Methods (Ch 17–21)

    CIA Chapter Topic Curriculum Coverage Rating
    Ch 17 Grilling, Broiling & Roasting Block 03-04 (dry heat overview) Adequate
    Ch 18 Sautéing, Pan Frying & Deep Frying Block 03-04, Block 05-06 (pan sauces) Adequate
    Ch 19 Steaming, Poaching & Submersion Block 03-04 (moist heat, briefly) Thin
    Ch 20 Braising & Stewing Block 03-04 + many protein blocks Strong
    Ch 21 (Garde manger methods) Covered in Block 27-28 Adequate

    Detailed notes:

    Ch 17 — Grilling, Broiling & Roasting. CIA recipe scope: grilled steaks with compound butter, sirloin with marchand de vin, beef teriyaki, pork chops with sherry vinegar sauce, lamb chops with cipollini onions, Indian grilled lamb with mango chutney, grilled chicken variations, grilled fish, spatchcocked birds.
    Block 03-04 addresses the principle of dry heat but reviews are primary. No session targets grilling variables specifically (direct vs. indirect heat, grill mark management, resting protocols, internal temperature targets by protein). This is foundational execution knowledge that learners self-discover late.

    Ch 18 — Sautéing, Pan Frying & Deep Frying. CIA covers: fond-based pan sauces (fines herbes sauce, sauce Robert, sauce charcutière), sauté of veal/pork scaloppine, trout amandine, trout meunière, bibimbap, stir-fried squid, vatapa. Deep frying is treated as a technique category (fish and chips, hush puppies, croquettes).
    Curriculum includes sautéed proteins but pan frying as a distinct method and deep frying as a category get no dedicated coverage. Learners encounter battered and fried items in the Japanese block (tempura) without prior framework.

    Ch 19 — Steaming, Poaching & Submersion. CIA covers: fish en papillote (bass with scallops, snapper with tropical garnish), poached sea bass with clams, poached trout with saffron mousse, sole paupiettes, shore dinner (court bouillon method), cioppino, Boston scrod with court bouillon, pescado veracruzana, poached chicken breast.
    The curriculum's moist-heat mentions in Block 03-04 are cursory. Court bouillon, en papillote, paupiettes, and submersion poaching are absent as teachable techniques. These appear incidentally in the French block (poached sole) without prior method grounding.

    Part IV – Cooking (Ch 22–30)

    CIA Chapter Topic Curriculum Coverage Rating
    Ch 22 Vegetables Block 11-12 full block Strong
    Ch 23 Potatoes Used as sides throughout; never taught Thin
    Ch 24 Grains & Legumes Block 13-14 (grains/risotto) Adequate
    Ch 25 Pasta & Dumplings Block 13-14 Adequate
    Ch 26 Eggs Block 07-08 full session Strong
    Ch 27 Salads & Dressings Block 05-06 (vinaigrette only) Thin
    Ch 28 Sandwiches Not covered (appropriate omission) —
    Ch 29 Hors d'Oeuvres & Appetizers Block 27-28 (charcuterie only) Thin
    Ch 30 Charcuterie & Garde Manger Block 27-28 Adequate

    Detailed notes:

    Ch 23 — Potatoes. CIA lists 20+ preparations: whipped, duchesse, boiled parsley, baked, Tuscan roasted, glazed sweet potato, gratin dauphinoise, Lyonnaise, château, Delmonico, hash brown, latkes, pommes Anna, macaire, rösti, French-fried, chips, soufflé potatoes, croquette, Lorette. Also: German potato salad and curried sweet potato salad.
    The curriculum uses "roasted potatoes" and "mashed potatoes" as default sides (appearing ~20 times combined) but never dedicates a session to potato technique. Learners never learn what makes a gratin different from a dauphinoise, or how resting post-boil affects mash texture.

    Ch 24 — Grains & Legumes. CIA scope: bean preparations (black beans 6 ways, frijoles refritos, chickpeas, white beans, lima beans, falafel, white bean stew, vegetarian chili), rice variations (pilaf — white, wild, wheat berry, barley, brown; arroz blanco, mexicano, brasileiro; coconut rice; risotto 5 variants including Milanese and mussels).
    Block 13-14 covers pasta, risotto, and some grains. Legumes as a topic — bean cookery, soaking vs. no-soak, dried vs. canned, falafel, refried beans from scratch — receive no dedicated session. They appear as side dishes in world cuisine blocks without instruction on the technique of cooking dried beans.

    Ch 25 — Pasta & Dumplings. CIA covers: fresh egg pasta in 10 color/flavor variants (whole wheat, buckwheat, spinach, saffron, citrus, curried, herbed, black pepper, red pepper, tomato, pumpkin/carrot/beet); carbonara, orecchiette with sausage and broccoli rabe, pad thai, glass noodles (japchae), tempeh cashew noodles, lasagna (Bolognese/béchamel and Napolitano), ravioli Bercy, gnocchi di semolina, gnocchi di ricotta, gnocchi Piedmontese, spaetzle, bread dumplings, biscuit dumplings, dim sum.
    Block 13-14 covers pasta well. Gaps: flavored pasta doughs (saffron, squid ink, beet) are not taught; spaetzle is absent; gnocchi is present but only in one form; dim sum dough appears in the Chinese block without prior dumpling method grounding.

    Ch 27 — Salads & Dressings. CIA teaches dressings as a systematic library: vinaigrettes (red wine, white wine, mustard-herb, roasted garlic-mustard, lemon-garlic, lemon-parsley, chipotle-sherry, almond-fig, apple cider, balsamic, curry, honey-poppy-citrus, fire-roasted tomato, guava-curry, truffle, pesto, vinaigrette gourmande, walnut oil — 18+ varieties). Also: Caesar, warm bacon dressing, composed salads, grain salads.
    The curriculum has a vinaigrette session in Block 05-06 and Caesar appears in multiple blocks. However, warm salads, grain salads, and the principle of emulsification ratios across dressing types are not taught. Salad appears throughout the curriculum as a side but not as a studied category.

    Ch 29 — Hors d'Oeuvres & Appetizers. CIA covers: mousses (salmon, blue cheese, goat cheese), salsas as a category (pico de gallo, verde asada, verde cruda, roja, papaya-black bean, grapefruit), Cumberland sauce, dipping sauces (Asian, Vietnamese, spring roll), condiments (yogurt cucumber, guacamole, hummus, baba ghanoush, harissa, tapenade, z'hug, wasabi cream, roasted red pepper marmalade, cranberry relish, fruit salsa).
    The curriculum touches spreads and condiments in world cuisine blocks (mezze in Block 41-42, salsas in Block 37-38) but treats them as recipe items within a cuisine context, not as a construction category. A learner doesn't learn the framework: spread vs. dip vs. cold mousse vs. composed bite.

    Ch 30 — Charcuterie & Garde Manger. CIA: seafood terrine, salmon flounder mousseline, pâté grand-mère, aspic, chicken & crayfish terrine, country-style pâté, pork roulade, chicken galantine, foie gras terrine, venison terrine, duck terrine with pistachios, chicken liver pâté, duck & smoked ham terrine, pâté en croûte, vegetable terrine, gravlax.
    Block 27-28 covers charcuterie and is one of the stronger specialty blocks. Gaps: gravlax (salt-curing), pâté en croûte, and vegetable terrines are not mentioned. Aspic and galantine are absent but their omission is defensible given scope.

    Part V – Baking (Ch 31–35)

    CIA Chapter Topic Curriculum Coverage Rating
    Ch 31 Baking MeP Block 45-46 intro Adequate
    Ch 32 Yeast Breads Block 45-46 full block Strong
    Ch 33 Pastry Doughs & Batters Block 47-48 Adequate
    Ch 34 Custards, Creams & Mousses Block 47-48 (partial) Adequate
    Ch 35 Fillings, Frostings & Dessert Sauces Block 47-48 (partial) Thin

    Detailed notes:

    Ch 33 — Pastry Doughs & Batters. CIA scope: basic pie dough (3-2-1 ratio), biscuits, cream scones, soda bread, pancakes/waffles (6 variants), puri, johnny cakes, crêpes suzette, puff pastry, blitz puff pastry, muffins (5 types), cornbread, banana-nut bread, pumpkin bread, pound cake, devil's food cake, angel food cake, vanilla sponge, chocolate sponge, cheesecake.
    Block 47-48 covers pastry doughs. Quick breads (biscuits, scones, muffins, cornbread) are a distinct technique category (chemical leaveners vs. yeast) that receives little explicit attention despite appearing in the block. Crêpes as a batter are absent from the curriculum entirely.

    Ch 35 — Fillings, Frostings & Dessert Sauces. CIA: Italian buttercream, fruit pies (apple, cherry, pecan, lemon meringue, pumpkin), frangipane tarts, ganache, chocolate sauce, sabayon/zabaglione, classic caramel sauce, raspberry coulis, apricot glaze, dried cherry sauce, apple butter, fruit salsa.
    The curriculum covers some desserts in Block 47-48 and Block 45-46 (desserts block). Dessert sauces as a technical category (caramel, coulis, sabayon, ganache) are not explicitly taught. A learner completing the block may know how to make crème brûlée without understanding the broader sauce family it implies.

    Part 2 — Meal Repetition Findings

    2.1 Structural Duplicate Blocks

    These blocks contain essentially the same session sequence as another block:

    Duplicate Pair Nature of Duplication
    15-16-beef + 17-18-beef All 10 sessions nearly identical (same mains, same sides, same skills focus)
    19-20-fish + 21-22-fish All 10 sessions nearly identical (crispy salmon, miso salmon, bouillabaisse, whole roasted fish repeated exactly)
    31-32-japanese + 33-34-japanese All 10 sessions identical

    These three pairs waste six weeks of a 52-week curriculum. The duplication appears to be from a copy-paste scaffolding error.

    2.2 Main Dish Repetition Across Non-Duplicate Blocks

    Main Dish Appearances Blocks
    Crispy pan-seared salmon 5× 03-04, 05-06, 07-08, 13-14, 19-20/21-22
    Braised short ribs 4× 03-04, 13-14, 15-16, 17-18
    Whole roasted / salt-baked fish 4× 09-10, 19-20, 21-22, 31-32 Italian
    Miso-glazed salmon 3× 03-04, 19-20, 21-22
    Mushroom risotto 3× 07-08, 13-14, 31-32 Italian
    Bouillabaisse 3× 07-08, 19-20, 21-22
    Gnocchi + brown butter sage 3× 09-10, 13-14, 31-32 Italian
    French onion soup 2× 01-02, 29-30 French
    Eggs Benedict + Hollandaise 2× 05-06, 29-30 French
    Ricotta ravioli + brown butter 2× 13-14, 31-32 Italian
    Pork chop + pan sauce 2× 05-06, 25-26 Pork
    Shakshuka 2× 01-02, 41-42 Middle Eastern
    Spatchcocked roast chicken 2× 03-04, 15-16

    2.3 Side Dish Monotony

    Side Estimated Frequency
    Simple green salad / vinaigrette ~28×
    Steamed / jasmine rice ~22×
    Crusty bread ~18×
    Roasted potatoes ~12×
    Arugula salad (parmesan/lemon) ~9×
    Mashed potatoes ~8×
    Asparagus ~6×
    Steamed bok choy ~5×

    The side dish menu is effectively three options rotating for the entire year. This also means potato technique (see CIA Ch 23) is never taught because the curriculum never requires it.

    Part 3 — Proposed Changes

    Organized by type and priority. All proposals are for curriculum changes only — no CIA recipes will be used. Free sources are suggested where links are needed.

    Priority 1 — Eliminate Structural Duplicates (6 weeks recovered)

    Proposal A: Collapse 15-16-beef + 17-18-beef into one beef block.
    Weeks 15-16 remain as Beef Fundamentals. Weeks 17-18 reuse the freed slot for a Lamb & Game block. CIA Ch 9 (Lamb) is one of the weakest areas in the curriculum — lamb currently appears only as a side note in Indian and Middle Eastern blocks. Content: braised lamb shanks (osso buco–style), lamb navarin, grilled lamb chops with herb crust, Indian spiced leg of lamb.
    Free sources: SE lamb shanks, SE grilled lamb chops.

    Proposal B: Make 19-20-fish and 21-22-fish distinct.
    Weeks 19-20: Flatfish & Round Fish (filleting, pan techniques, meunière, crudo). Weeks 21-22: Whole fish, curing, and advanced seafood (gravlax, bouillabaisse, shellfish cookery).
    This also absorbs the current separate 25-26-shellfish block gap (shellfish appears in neither current 19-20 nor 21-22 with meaningful distinction).

    Proposal C: Collapse 31-32-japanese + 33-34-japanese into one Japan block.
    Weeks 31-32 become Japan. Weeks 33-34 become Korea (currently completely absent from the curriculum despite CIA Ch 20 including Korean braised short rib, bibimbap, japchae). Content: bibimbap, japchae, Korean fried chicken, doenjang jjigae, Korean BBQ fundamentals.
    Free sources: Maangchi bibimbap, Maangchi doenjang jjigae, SE Korean fried chicken.

    Priority 2 — Deduplicate Main Dishes

    Proposal D: Replace 4 redundant crispy salmon appearances.
    Keep salmon in Weeks 05-06 (as a pan technique showcase with a simple pan sauce). Remove or replace the other four appearances:
    - Block 03-04: replace with spatchcocked chicken (already in curriculum once — move here instead)
    - Block 07-08: replace with steamed fish in court bouillon or poached salmon with beurre blanc (introduces CIA Ch 19 technique)
    - Block 13-14: replace with pasta with seafood (already a pasta block — linguine alle vongole fits)
    - Blocks 19-20 / 21-22: salmon belongs in fish blocks but should be one method per block — 19-20 keeps miso-glazed salmon; 21-22 uses whole roasted or salt-baked fish

    Proposal E: Cut braised short ribs to one appearance.
    Keep in Block 03-04 (exemplary braising introduction). Remove from 13-14, 15-16, 17-18. Replace:
    - Block 13-14 (pasta/grains): osso buco (veal cross-cut, braised, served over risotto Milanese — CIA classic not currently in curriculum)
    - Block 15-16 (beef fund.): Yankee pot roast or beef rouladen — different braise profiles teaching pot-roast vs. wrapped-and-tied method respectively
    - Block 17-18 (opens up for lamb per Proposal A)
    Free sources: SE osso buco, SE pot roast.

    Proposal F: Cut bouillabaisse to one appearance.
    Keep in Block 07-08 (exemplifies stock + finishing technique). In fish blocks, replace second and third instances with: cioppino (American equivalent, different flavor profile) and a whole-fish braise (Indonesian or Portuguese style — new technique territory).

    Proposal G: Cut mushroom risotto to one role.
    Keep in Block 13-14 as the pasta/grains block anchor. Remove from Block 07-08 (replace with risotto Milanese — saffron + bone marrow, teaches a different risotto variant) and Block 31-32 Italian (replace with pasta al forno or cacio e pepe — different pasta techniques).

    Priority 3 — Add Missing Technique Blocks

    Proposal H: Add a Soups Architecture session (within existing Block 09-10 Seasoning & Flavor, or Block 02 of any protein block).
    CIA Ch 14 teaches soups as a taxonomy: broth → cream soup (roux-based) → purée (vegetable) → bisque (crustacean + cream) → chowder (potato-thickened) → consommé (clarified). One session on this architecture would retroactively explain all the soups learners have been making.
    Structure: Session covers the four "base" types and demonstrates a cream soup (broccoli), a purée (butternut squash), and cold technique (gazpacho for summer / vichyssoise for winter).
    Free sources: SE butternut squash soup, SE gazpacho, SE consommé method.

    Proposal I: Add a Potato Technique session (within Block 11-12 Vegetables or as a standalone skills session).
    The entire year treats potatoes as a side filler. CIA Ch 23 shows 20+ preparations. One session covering three families: mashed family (whipped, duchess, croquette — the texture spectrum), dry-heat family (roasted, pommes Anna, rösti, gratin dauphinoise), and fried family (hash brown, latkes) would make potatoes teachable for the rest of the curriculum.
    Free sources: SE perfect mashed potatoes, SE gratin dauphinoise, SE rösti, SE latkes.

    Proposal J: Expand Block 05-06 sauces to include compound butters and beurre blanc.
    CIA Ch 13 lists 8 compound butters (maître d'hôtel, tarragon, pimiento, green onion, dill, sun-dried tomato, basil, plus the butter log method). Beurre blanc is the emulsified butter sauce that bridges the French block and the fish blocks.
    One half-session addition to Block 05-06: make 2-3 compound butters and one beurre blanc — then these appear naturally as sauce choices in every subsequent protein block.
    Free sources: SE beurre blanc, SE compound butters.

    Proposal K: Add Steaming & En Papillote to Block 03-04 moist heat.
    Currently, court bouillon and en papillote are absent from the curriculum. CIA Ch 19 shows these are a natural family with poaching. One session addition to Block 03-04: en papillote method (fish or chicken breast), and poaching in court bouillon. This builds the vocabulary needed when poached sole appears in the French block.
    Free sources: SE en papillote, SE court bouillon poached fish.

    Proposal L: Add a Spice Blends & Marinades session to Block 09-10 (Seasoning & Flavor).
    CIA Ch 15 teaches spice blends and marinades as a library, not as incidentals. Block 09-10 is "Seasoning & Flavor" — currently underspecified. Adding: dry rub construction (salt + heat + aroma ratios), wet marinade acid/oil ratio, curry paste making (one each red/green base), and a fines herbes vs. bouquet garni distinction would prepare learners for all Module 4 world cuisine blocks.
    Free sources: SE dry rub guide, SE garam masala from scratch, Woks of Life curry paste.

    Proposal M: Add Dessert Sauces to Block 47-48 (Pastry).
    CIA Ch 35 covers: caramel sauce, chocolate sauce (ganache-based), pastry cream, sabayon/zabaglione, raspberry coulis, and apricot glaze. These are the toolkit for plating any pastry item.
    A half-session: make one fat-based sauce (ganache), one egg-based sauce (sabayon), one fruit sauce (coulis or coulis variation). All three techniques transfer to any dessert.
    Free sources: SE caramel sauce, SE chocolate ganache, SE sabayon.

    Priority 4 — Side Dish Diversification

    The current rotation (green salad / rice / crusty bread) needs systematic replacement. The following substitution table can be applied across all blocks without requiring new skill sessions:

    Current Default Side Proposed Replacement Pool
    "Simple green salad" Warm salad with bacon vinaigrette; grain salad (farro, freekeh, or wheat berry); fattoush; panzanella; composed salad with protein elements
    "Steamed rice" Rice pilaf (toasted, flavored with aromatics); freekeh pilaf; mujaddara; arroz mexicano; coconut rice
    "Crusty bread" Grissini; cornbread; fougasse; potato bread; roti (Indian blocks specifically)
    "Roasted potatoes" Gratin dauphinoise (in French block); pommes Anna (special occasion); rösti (breakfast blocks); patatas bravas (Spanish / Mexican adjacents)
    "Mashed potatoes" Duchesse piped (skill builder); white bean purée; cauliflower purée; parsnip mash

    Rule of thumb: No side dish should appear in more than 4 consecutive blocks; no side dish should appear more than 8 times total in the curriculum.

    Priority 5 — Absent Protein Types

    Proposal N: Add Duck to either Block 27-28 (Charcuterie) or Block 29-30 (French).
    Duck confit is CIA Ch 20 anchor content and is one of the most educational preparations in the French kitchen (teaches fat-submersion cooking, preservation, rendering). Duck breast à l'orange or duck breast with cherry sauce are achievable learner targets.
    Free sources: SE duck confit, SE duck breast, SE duck à l'orange.

    Proposal O: Add Offal/Variety Meats to the Charcuterie block or as a stretch session.
    CIA covers: chicken liver pâté, foie gras terrine. The curriculum has no offal work whatsoever. A single session on chicken livers (pâté, dirty rice, crostini topping) would fulfill a meaningful gap without cost or difficulty barriers.
    Free sources: SE chicken liver mousse, SE dirty rice.

    Priority 6 — Korea as a Missing Cuisine (per Proposal C)

    Korea is one of the most influential culinary forces of the 21st century and is absent from the curriculum. CIA Ch 18 already includes bibimbap and CIA Ch 20 includes Korean braised short ribs, signaling the CIA judged it significant enough even in 2011.
    Proposed Block 33-34 Korea curriculum anchor sessions:
    - Session 1: bibimbap (banchan construction, dolsot technique, gochujang sauce)
    - Session 2: japchae + Korean fried chicken (two technique modes: glass noodle stir-fry and double-fry batter)
    - Session 3: doenjang jjigae (fermented paste soup — teaches the fermented pantry)
    - Session 4: Korean BBQ fundamentals (bulgogi, galbi) + kimchi as a pantry skill
    - Session 5: Full meal — Korean BBQ spread with banchan, soup, rice

    Priority 7 — Food Safety & Nutrition (Foundational Content)

    These two topics are effectively absent from the curriculum:

    Food Safety — CIA Ch 4 covers HACCP principles, temperature danger zones, cross-contamination, safe thawing, holding temperatures. The current curriculum has one FDA link in Block 01-02. Proposed: a food safety "primer" document (not a full session, but a standalone reference in Block 01-02's quick-ref) covering: danger zone thermometers, FIFO, cross-contamination protocol, and the key internal temperatures by protein.

    Nutrition — CIA Ch 3 covers macro ratios, dietary modifications, and reading a nutrition label. Given the scope, this is probably out of scope for this curriculum. No proposal is made to add it.
